How Far-Reaching is Socrates' Argument at Euthyphro 10a-11b?

Michael Winter

University of St. Thomas

1995

 

This paper will partially address an issue that arises in connection with a problem in Plato's Euthyphro.  I am interested in the particular argument that Socrates uses to reduce Euthyphro's definition of 'piety' as 'what is loved by the gods' to absurdity.  S. Marc Cohen presents what I regard to be the most thorough and compelling interpretations of the argument in the Euthyphro to date.  I am less concerned with a detailed look at Cohen's analysis than with considering the extent to which the argument presented in the Euthyphro is universalizable.  In particular, I am interested in considering a simple believer, one whose normative ethical theory is authoritarian.  What are the ramifications of substituting the simple believer's 'right action' for Euthyphro's 'piety'?  How well does Socrates' argument work against the simple believer's definition of 'right action' as 'action commanded by God'?  It will be interesting to look at some implications that might follow from this substitution.  If the argument form presented in the Euthyphro works against the proposed definition of 'right action', then it is worth looking at some assumptions that must accompany the argument.  I will examine some of these assumptions and try to decide whether or not the simple believer portrayed in the Old Testament should accept them.  If the argument in the Euthyphro is universalizable in any interesting way, it must meet the minimal condition of being valid.  Cohen's analysis is useful because it is the only satisfactory interpretation of Socrates' argument that I am aware of that accounts for the argument's universalizability and validity.

 

     In considering whether Cohen's conclusion about the universalizability of the argument forces the simple believer to abandon his position or accept some serious constraints, ultimately I hope to show that a distinction drawn by St. Thomas Aquinas enables the simple believer to free himself from the clutches of Socrates' argument as Cohen presents it.  In light of Aquinas' idea about the divine mode of knowing, I argue that the simple believer can plausibly reject at least one assumption that must accompany a universalized version of the argument.  The suggestion I offer is that the simple believer can deny one of the key premises of the modified argument without being committed to the view that God's will could be arbitrary.

 

PRELIMINARIES

Euthyphro proposes two definitions of 'piety' prior to the one that motivates Socrates' argument.  He first suggests at (5d-e) that piety is what he is doing, i.e. prosecuting those who are guilty of murder and sacrilege regardless of who it may be.  Socrates, as is expected, is not interested in particular instances of piety; what he is looking for is that in virtue of which all pious actions are pious.  The second proposal is that piety is what is dear to the gods. (7a)But such a proposal cannot be right because Euthyphro is willing to assert that the gods quarrel, and can thereby disagree about what is pious.  So, on this account, the same things will be pious and impious.(8a-b)In light of this implication, Euthyphro qualifies his definition with Socrates' assistance.  His third proposal is that 'pious' is 'what all the gods love'.(9e)

     Socrates attempts to show that if Euthyphro accepts this third proposal, he will be committed to an inconsistency.  Socrates' argument is intended to demonstrate that 'piety' cannot mean 'loved by the gods' whether it be some or all of them.  Socrates presents the following disjunction to Euthyphro since he is not quite sure what Euthyphro intends with his definition:

 

D: Either what is pious is loved by the gods because it is pious or it is pious because it is loved by the gods.

Euthyphro is not sure what to say, so Socrates attempts to make clearer the distinction he has in mind.  Unfortunately, the discussion that immediately follows is quite complicated and confusing.  It appears at this stage of the argument that D is exclusive and exhaustive, but since the portion of the dialogue that follows is opaque, it is probably best to get a clearer idea about what follows before drawing the conclusion that the disjunction should be read exclusively and exhaustively.

Since I am primarily interested in addressing the issue of whether or not an argument patterned after Socrates' argument in the Euthyphro tells against what one might call a "Divine Command" theory of morality, I will treat Socrates' argument with the following substitutions:

 

  • 'Right action' for 'Piety'
  • 'Action commanded by God' for 'What the gods love'
  • 'God-commanded' for 'god-loved'

Since Cohen deals with Socrates' argument, I will consider these substitutions in relation to his analysis.  I am interested in considering the implications surrounding an argument like Socrates' that targets someone who claims that an action is right just in case it is commanded by God.  In keeping with this approach, it is useful to replace Euthyphro and Socrates with characters that one can call "the simple believer" and "the DCM challenger" respectively where relevant.  What does the argument look like?

THE ARGUMENT

The proponent of DCM, i.e. the simple believer, following Euthyphro, would accept the following two claims:

 

(1) What is right is commanded by God because it is right.

(2) What is God-commanded is God-commanded because it is commanded by God.

(1) corresponds to the first disjunct of D above, which is accepted by Euthyphro after an explication from Socrates.  The explication is of interest to Cohen.  On the basis of it Euthyphro acknowledges that the following two sentences are obtained by a straightforward substitution:

 

(3) What is God-commanded is commanded by God because it is God-commanded. (from 1)

(4) What is right is right because it is commanded by God. (from 2)

 

The general picture is that (1) and (2) appear true while (3) and (4) appear false.  In order to make it clear how these substitutions are warranted, the DCM challenger must point out that 'right action' is supposed to mean 'God-commanded' action by the simple believer's hypothesis.  The point is made by drawing a distinction between different tenses of verbs.

It is quite important to note at this stage, however, that the simple believer portrayed in the Old Testament would be more willing to accept (4) than (1); one might expect that Euthyphro should be too.  Why does he end up rejecting the counterpart of (4)?It is not possible to give an adequate account of this matter without introducing some further distinctions.  Cohen argues that 'because' (hoti) is used in two senses, that the gods' love of piety is rational, and that there is an important conceptual truth that, when understood, renders D exclusive and exhaustive.  These three points considered collectively offer an explanation of Euthyphro's acceptance of (1) and rejection of (4).  Much more will be said about these claims soon.

 

COHEN'S INTERPRETATION

Cohen first explains that the proponent need not reject the second disjunct of D; in fact he cannot deny it if he is to be consistent with his definition.[1]Cohen is interested in the argument that purports to show that (2) is true while (3) is false, which is partially motivated by an examination of passive verb forms in the argument.  Socrates first distinguishes between active and passive participles.  For example, our use of speech manifests a distinction between someone's carrying a thing and its being carried or something's carrying a thing and its being carried.  The difference is plain enough here.  But Socrates' examples then appeal to the contrast between the passive participle and the inflected third person singular passive.[1]Since the first distinction is between an active and a passive form, while the second is between two passive forms, it is not clear how the two cases relate.  Furthermore, in the second case, both forms are usually translated into English in the same way.  Cohen suggests that the use of the passive participle in Socrates' argument might initially be thought of as explicating the notion of alteration.  But since the relevant argument is about love, and the object of love is not altered by the subject's love, it is not correct to interpret the distinction in this way.

     It turns out that the argument only makes sense if 'because' (hoti) is used equivocally.[1]Cohen suggests that 'because' might introduce a reason for something or it might introduce a logically sufficient condition.  If  ø is a verb, one might interpret Socrates' distinction in the following way:

a) Someone or something ø-s a thing because it is a ø-ed thing.

The most natural reading of (a) in the context of the argument indicates that 'because' (hoti) is introducing a reason for something.  If one is interested in substituting the verb 'love' in (a), then "the reason introduced will have to be a person's reason for having a certain attitude or performing a certain action."[1]

The other sense of the term 'because' is utilized in this sentence form:

b) Something is a ø-d thing because someone or something ø-s it.

In (b) it is fairly obvious that 'because' is not introducing a reason as it was in (a); it is rather introducing a logically sufficient condition.[1]One should read (b) as answering the question "Why is something a ø-d thing?"

If this distinction is correct, then not only is the term 'because' used equivocally, but there are two distinct senses of the term that give rise to the suggested interpretations of the sentence forms above.  It remains to be seen whether Socrates' argument in the Euthyphro commits the fallacy of equivocation.  In order to find out, all one need do is apply the appropriate terms in light of this distinction and look for the fallacy.  If one applies the appropriate distinctions to (1)-(4), one gets the following:

(1*) The reason God commands what is right is because it is right.

(2*) A logically sufficient condition for applying the term 'God-commanded' to what is God-commanded is that it is commanded by God.

(3*) The reason God commands what is God-commanded is that it is God-commanded.

(4*) A logically sufficient condition for applying the term 'right' to what is right is that God commands it.

Note that the term 'because' is used univocally in (1*) and (3*) as well as in (2*) and (4*)-- there is no fallacy of equivocation.  Cohen argues that one should accept (1*) and (2*), but reject (3*) and (4*).Understood in this way, Cohen thinks that the DCM challenger's argument would show that if God's reason for commanding what is right is that it is right, then 'right action' cannot be defined as 'God-commanded action'.  In other words if God has a reason for commanding what is right, one should appeal to this reason in trying to define 'right action'.

It is quite important to note that this interpretation presupposes that God's commanding an act is rational.  This means that God has reasons for what he does; but this is not enough.  In order for the argument to go through, God's reasons for commanding actions must be rooted in the nature of what it is for something to be right.  Moreover, Cohen takes what follows to be a conceptual truth:

 

C: If x's reason for ø-ing y is that y has the property P, then it must be possible for y to have property P independently of being ø-ed by x.[1]

Cohen thinks that the denial of C would entail that x's reason would be no reason at all.  It is important to recognize that if C is true, then D above is both exclusive and exhaustive.  One virtue of Cohen's analysis is its ability to explain the distinction between something's being carried and someone's carrying it, and how this leads to Euthyphro's acceptance of (a) and rejection of (b).  Another is that it explains how the argument does involve an equivocal use of the term 'because' but that it does not involve the fallacy of equivocation.  A third is its proposal of a plausible way of generalizing the argument.A last is its presentation of a conceptual truth that accounts for the intuitive force of D.  In light of this impressive, if complicated, interpretation of Socrates' argument, what should be said regarding the simple believer?

    The simple believer portrayed in the Old Testament takes God's commands as a standard of morality: the fact that God commands an action makes the action right.  Since the simple believer would probably be more willing to accept (4), it is plausible to assume that he would less likely to accept (1) or (1*).  If Cohen's analysis is correct, however, the simple believer's acceptance of (4) or (4*) entails the rejection of (1) or (1*) because of the exclusive and exhaustive nature of D.  In making this move, it appears that the simple believer would have to be willing to deny that God's commanding what is right would necessarily be rational.  This seems to leave open the possibility that God could command an action arbitrarily and that it would still be right to follow such a command.  To the extent that the simple believer adheres to a divine command theory of morality, must he be open to accepting the possibility that commands from God need not be rational?  The general conclusion that Cohen arrives at on the basis of his analysis is quite relevant to the simple believer's acceptance or rejection of (4) or (4*).

 

            If a moral concept M is such that there is an authority whose judgment whether or not something falls under M is decisive and rationally grounded, then 'M' cannot be defined in terms of that authority's judgment.[1]

The simple believer's adherence to DCM commits him to normative and metaethical theories that are authoritarian.  Is the proponent of the Divine Command theory forced to abandon his authoritarian conception of morality? 

 

DEFENSE OF DCM

At this stage it looks as though the best available interpretation of Socrates' argument in the Euthyphro forces the simple believer to either abandon DCM to the extent that it claims that God's will is the standard of morality, or else weaken DCM by allowing for the possibility that God might command certain acts arbitrarily.  The simple believer is presented with these alternatives in light of the fact that the conceptual truth C above makes the disjunction D both exclusive and exhaustive.  Since DCM, by its very nature, claims that God's will is the standard of morality, the proponent of DCM would plausibly accept either (4) or (4*).The acceptance of either (4) or (4*) entails the rejection of (1) or (1*).For this reason, it appears as though the simple believer is committed to saying that arbitrary commands would be right to the extent that they are willed by God.  If the latter price is too high, must the simple believer abandon DCM?

    A defense of the simple believer's adherence to DCM requires careful examination of some of the key points in Cohen's argument.   There seem to be at least three theses that might be attacked.  The first is that 'because' (hoti) is used equivocally in the argument; it is this observation that allows Cohen to introduce the distinction between 'because' as a reason and 'because' as a sufficient condition.  It does not seem, however, that Cohen is going out on a limb here.  Other treatments of the argument rely on the very same point.[1]It is a fact about our language that the term 'because' introduces intentional contexts.  There seems to be at least some degree of consensus among commentators that some distinction about 'because' is necessary in the argument.

One might question whether or not God's commands are rational.  The rationality of the gods is a crucial assumption in Cohen's interpretation of Socrates' argument.  Even though this premise is nowhere stated in the text of the Euthyphro, one might think that there is indirect evidence for the premise in light of the difference between Euthyphro's first and second attempts to define 'piety' in terms of what the gods love.  Since Euthyphro's second attempt at a definition of 'piety' involves the gods' agreement, it is reasonable to think that Euthyphro did in fact think that the gods were rational; on what other basis could they agree about anything?  Suppose that Euthyphro does in fact think that the gods are rational, does the simple believer have to make the same concession?  I am not sure that he does.  The reason for this is that it is not at all obvious that Euthyphro's anthropomorphic conception of the gods' rationality is the same as the simple believer's perception of God's rationality.  The point deserves more careful attention.

     Even though it is not certain that Euthyphro does in fact think that the gods are rational, since he does not explicitly state that they are, let us assume for the sake of argument that he did think they were.  If this is so, then Cohen's analysis of at least part of the argument is correct by hypothesis.  In this case it is clear that the conception of rationality that Euthyphro and Cohen have in mind is the same, ordinary one.  Remember, this conception of rationality portrays the gods as having reasons for what they command and love and that these reasons are such that they must be rooted in the nature of the property in virtue of which something is commanded or loved.  Though this conception of rationality is a plausible characterization of any normal human being's mode of thinking or, for that matter, any of Euthyphro's gods, why should one assume that this conception correctly describes the way God, according to the Judeo-Christian tradition, would reason?  If the simple believer's God does indeed exist, then this Being is radically different from and superior to human beings and the gods of the ancient mythology.         Would it not be reasonable to expect that God's mode of knowing would be as unique as His mode of existence?  Cohen does not take this possibility seriously, but if plausible, it suggests that 'rationality' might have another sense when applied to God.

If one is open to this possibility, there is at least the potential for criticizing another important assumption in Cohen's argument, i.e. if something is not rational in the sense Cohen has in mind, then it is irrational.  If the term 'rationality' is confined to a characterization of how human thinking operates, then Cohen's assumption is quite reasonable, but if it is possible to describe a mode of reasoning that is notably different from that of humans, and this mode fits well within the simple believer's view of the world, then there could be a sense of 'rationality' that would leave the simple believer's view of morality largely unaffected by the force of the present argument directed against him.

 

DOES GOD THINK LIKE WE DO?

In attempting to formulate some sort of reply on the simple believer's behalf, it is important to note at the outset that the simple believer's worldview is different from the non-believer's.  One obvious difference between a believer's and a non-believer's way of looking at the world is the fact that the believer thinks that there is a perfect Being who is responsible for his existence.Part of the appeal of Cohen's interpretation of Socrates' argument is that Cohen is at least implicitly willing to accept the simple believer's DCM on its own terms-- the conclusion of the argument is universalizable.  In defending the simple believer against Cohen's impressive objection, I am going to point out a way of thinking about the divine mode of knowing which, I suggest, fits well within the simple believer's world-view and offers reason to doubt that 'rationality' in the sense that applies to human discursive thinking can capture all that God's mode of thought encompasses.  This move should allow the simple believer to reject a key assumption in Cohen's argument, i.e., that God is rational.  The picture of rationality involved in this assumption might be at odds with the simple believer's conception of God.  Since Cohen's argument will not work without this key assumption, a rejection of it, if plausible, would lead to a successful defense of DCM against this particular attack.I suggest that some such move can be made without committing the simple believer to the position that God's commands could be arbitrary.

A simple believer should be committed to the view that God is perfect-- most Judeo-Christian conceptions of God include His perfection.  If God is perfect, then one might apply a bit of Aristotelian terminology to describe His nature: God is pure actuality, and His nature therefore contains no potentiality.  St. Thomas Aquinas develops a theory of God's nature and knowledge that employs the distinction just suggested.

 

            Since therefore God has nothing in Him of potentiality, but is pure act, His intellect and its object must be altogether the same; so that He neither is without the intelligible species, as is the case with our intellect when it understands potentially, nor does the intelligible species differ from the substance of the divine intellect, as it differs in our intellect when it understands actually; but the intelligible species itself is the divine intellect itself, and thus God understands Himself through Himself.[1][1]

Aquinas portrays a view of God's knowledge in which there is no distinction between knower and thing known.  According to this line, if the object of knowledge is distinct from the knower, then it is possible that the knower will be in a state of potentiality with respect to the object.  But since God is perfect, and therefore pure act, this separation is not possible with respect to His knowledge.  Human understanding, on the other hand, is imperfect, and involves potentiality to the extent that it is.  Aquinas' proposal suggests a significant difference between God's knowledge and ours; while each requires an object, the object of God's knowledge is the divine substance itself.  Since knower and thing known are the same in God's case according to this picture, His knowledge can be perfect.  With human beings, however, the object of knowledge is distinct from the knowing faculty, hence human knowledge is imperfect. 

How do these considerations apply to Cohen's argument?  As I mentioned, it seems that Cohen's conception of rationality is confined to the type of knowledge that human beings have.  If Cohen's argument is interpreted in the context of DCM as it has been throughout much of this paper, there are two explicit assumptions made: that God has reasons for commanding what He commands, and that His reasons are rooted in the nature of the property commanded, i.e. rightness.  Human knowledge is discursive, and can as a result be described as involving both potency and actuality.  Human thinking is well-described by Cohen's assumptions.  But if it plausible to think that God's knowledge is superior to and different from ours, so different that it lacks any imperfection, then it makes sense to think that there could be a unique concept of rationality corresponding to this type of knowledge. God might have reasons for commanding what He does, but if the above characterization is right, then His reasons cannot be independent of His substance.  Since God is one, it must follow that His will and His intellect are not distinct.  So if the object of God's knowledge is His substance, then the object of His will is too.  If this is right, then it would at least follow that God cannot will or think of anything that is distinct from His substance.  But these features are not imperfections any more than God's inability to square a circle is.

     If this suggestion is remotely plausible, then there are a few points that are worth considering.  First, there is no good reason to think that God reasons like we do.  Indeed, the simple believer's world-view portrays a Being that embodies perfection; on this basis, it is reasonable to think that God would understand perfectly.  Second, if God does understand perfectly according to the simple believer, then one should look for the best explanation of what this might amount to.  Aquinas' account employs a reasonable distinction that suggests a minimal account of what the divine mode of knowing must include.  Third, the simple believer or any proponent of DCM is warranted in adopting this explanation in so far as it fits with and explains his position.  It is the advocate of this position who is under attack.  Fourth, the challenger of DCM who employs Socrates' argument, i.e. Cohen, should be willing to allow the proponent of DCM to adopt this distinction since it fits reasonably well with this view; Cohen is apparently willing to challenge the latter position on its own terms.  Fifth, since, according to this view, God's mode of understanding might be radically different from ours, the term 'rational' in the sense Cohen has in mind is not obviously applicable to God.  It is not obvious that if God has reasons for what He does that these reasons are somehow independent from Him.  If Aquinas is right, this way of thinking about God is quite misleading.  It is eminently reasonable to suppose that God's thought is governed by the laws of logic, in this way His thinking is similar to ours, but since His nature contains no potentiality, the object of His thought cannot be distinct form the divine essence.  Human, discursive thinking is probably correctly portrayed by Cohen's view of it, but it just does not follow that God's thinking does.  Sixth, it does not follow from the fact that God is not rational in the way we are that He is irrational.  His perfection rules this possibility out.  Therefore, armed with the reply I have suggested, there is no obvious reason to think that the simple believer should be committed to a position that allows God's commands to be arbitrary.

What about the conceptual truth C?  It is not easy to think of a counterexample to it if the principle is accepted on Cohen's terms.  If the considerations above make sense, however, there is a way of rejecting Cohen's terms.  From God's reason for commanding actions, i.e. that they are right, it doesn't necessarily follow that it is possible for actions to be right independently of God's commanding them.  The object of God's will is not independent of His power of willing according to Aquinas' suggestion.If it is reasonable for the simple believer to adhere to ideas about God's perfection and an explanation of His mode of knowing that Aquinas addresses, then C does not cover the case of God.  It does not follow from this that God might command things arbitrarily, for this to be true God would have to irrational.  But as was already suggested, this alternative is less plausible than others that are available.  It does not look as though C is exhaustive after all; the divine mode of understanding probably does not fall under it.

     What about rejecting Aquinas' idea about the divine mode of knowing?  It is worth noting that there is no immediate evidence that suggests that Aquinas invokes this distinction to account for a problem like this; it does not look like an ad hoc distinction.  Moreover,  it seems like the simple believer is completely justified in adopting this distinction if his adherence to DCM is under attack.  The distinction makes sense within the simple believer's conceptual framework.  Cohen should allow the proponent of DCM to adopt this distinction since his interpretation of Socrates' argument is universalizable; this means that it is applicable to any theory that alleges that a value term can be defined in terms of a psychological state.  DCM is one such theory, and what distinguishes it from other theories of this kind is that it involves a simple believer's God.This is the point that, I suggest, forces Cohen to accept the theory on its own terms.

 

CONCLUDING REMARKS

What I hope to have shown is that one's normative and metaethical views might both be authoritarian if the authority is so unique that His mode of knowing is perfect in the sense that there is no distinction between knower and thing known in His case.  One might question whether a Being fitting this description is even possible.  There doesn't seem to be any contradiction in supposing that there is.  One might doubt that any such Being does in fact exist, but this issue seems to be beside the point.  Since Socrates' argument is supposed to be universalizable in a particular way according to Cohen's interpretation, it is reasonable to think a Divine Command theory is an intended target.  This suggests that the challenger is willing to accept such a theory on its own terms.  It might be objected that the simple believer's beliefs are supposed to be simple, and for this reason, a distinction like Aquinas' would not be one that he would be likely to hold.  The point is probably correct, but the fact that the simple believer is subject to a serious and powerful objection should motivate him to accept any considerations that might fill out his conception of God.  I think that Aquinas' distinction does just this.  In adopting a distinction like Aquinas', the simple believer's ideas about morality might seem less simple, but there is nothing that forces one to say that his beliefs are any less so.
