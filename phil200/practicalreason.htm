Practical Reason and a Demonstrative Science of Aristotle's Ethics

Michael Winter

 

      I wish to explore the extent to which Aristotelian practical reason (phronesis) is compatible with a deductive construal of Aristotle’s ethical theory.  We might be inclined to think that the subtleties of a virtue-based theory of ethics cannot be captured in any set of general principles or formulas because of the predominant role that particular circumstances play in determining what it would be virtuous to do. As a result, we may think that a deductive paradigm of ethics cannot adequately capture how virtuous agents reason about action.[1]  A deductive paradigm of ethics would seem to require a kind of precision in matters of conduct that Aristotle warns us against seeking.  Moreover, Aristotle’s frequent claims in the Nicomachean Ethics about ethical subject-matter holding for the most part speak against the prospects for understanding his ethical theory according to a deductive paradigm.

     I am not persuaded that Aristotle’s remarks about imprecision in ethics rule out a deductive construal of his ethics.  In this paper I will present a partial case for considering ethics as an Aristotelian science.  I sketch an interpretation of for the most part (hos epi to polu) relations in Aristotle that allows for propositions expressing these relations to be components of demonstrative syllogisms.  After presenting these components of a general case for an Aristotelian science of ethics, I argue that an  important part of what a phronimos knows can be captured by specifiable principles that hold for the most part.  I think that there is much to be gained from understanding Aristotle’s ethics deductively, and I am not convinced that a deductive understanding of Aristotle’s ethics minimizes or undermines the role of phronesis in Aristotle’s theory.

 

 

 

Imprecision in Aristotle’s ethics

     Although Aristotle does not present a deductive paradigm of ethics in his ethical treatises, it is important to think about the extent to which it is appropriate to consider a portion of Aristotle’s ethics from this perspective. The fact that Aristotle does not present a deductive paradigm of biology in his biological treatises, for example, does not rule out the possibility of treating biology as a legitimate Aristotelian science.  If there are persuasive reasons for thinking that a significant portion of Aristotle’s ethics could fit the paradigm of science presented in the Posterior Analytics, such a model of ethics may provide an impressive picture of moral realism.  Also, one might wonder about whether Aquinas’ natural law theory of ethics is thoroughly Aristotelian.  A deductive paradigm of Aristotle’s ethics would provide one way of seeing how Aquinas’ theory could be viewed as an extension of Aristotle’s.

     However, before looking at some of the components that this approach to Aristotle would be likely to include, it is imperative to say something about some of the remarks that Aristotle makes in the early portions of the Nicomachean Ethics that seemingly undermine this project before it gets underway.  Since some of these remarks might be taken to rule out a deductive interpretation of  Aristotle’s ethics, I need to point out that these remarks need not be read as ruling this possibility out.  

     Consider the following passage:

              Since, then, the present inquiry does not aim at theoretical knowledge like the others (for we are inquiring not in order to know what excellence is, but in order to become good, since otherwise our inquiry would have been of no use), we must examine the nature of actions, namely how we ought to do them; for these determine also the nature of the states that are produced, as we have said....But this must be agreed upon beforehand, that the whole account of matters of conduct must be given in outline and not precisely, as we said at the very beginning that the accounts we demand must be in accordance with the subject-matter; matters concerned with conduct and questions of what is good for us have no fixity, any more than matters of health.  The general account being of this nature, the account of particular cases is yet more lacking in exactness; for they do not fall under any art or set of precepts, but the agents themselves must in each case consider what is appropriate to the occasion, as happens also in the art of medicine or of navigation. (Nicomachean Ethics 1103b27-1104a9)

     It is significant to note the phrase "present inquiry" in the first line.  It may well be that the coments that follow it are intended to characterize the sort of inquiry that Aristotle intends to carry out in the Nicomachean Ethics, but not the only one for which the subject-matter leaves room.  Remarks about imprecision in ethics are virtually absent from Aristotle’s Eudemian Ethics.  The formal character of the Eudemian Ethics by comparison to the relatively informal character of the Nicomachean Ethics leaves us much room to wonder about the extent to which ethics admits of formal treatment.

     Moreover, in the third line of the passage, Aristotle says that if the present inquiry were to be theoretical it would be useless.  It may indeed be useless with respect to some purposes, i.e., the ones that Aristotle pursues in the NE, and not for others, e.g., the ones pursued in the EE.  Aristotle's stated purpose in the Nicomachean Ethics is to enable his student to "become good", and a theoretical or scientific treatment of the subject-matter is probably inappropriate for his audience.  It may prove useful to distinguish between the immediate and ultimate goals of ethical inquiry.[2]  To say that ethics is a practical discipline is to say that its ultimate goals are practical--making its students better people.  But to say that ethics has an ultimate goal that is non-cognitive in character says nothing about the nature of subordinate goals that may be pursued in ethics.  It is conceivable that there are cognitive goals that are pursued for the sake of non-cognitive goals, or that cognitive goals could be studied for its own sake.

     To elaborate on this point, consider the comparison between ethics and medicine that Aristotle makes toward the end of the passage.  Given that the ultimate goal of medicine is to make people healthy, medical science would be rightly classified as a practical discipline.  But does the fact that the ultimate goal of medicine is practical entail that there are not any other goals within medicine that may be cognitive?  Obviously not.  If there is a parallel between ethics and medicine, as Aristotle thinks there is, then we could expect that the character of goals and the relation between goals within each discipline would be similar.  Both ethics and medicine have ultimate goals that are non-cognitive and practical, but both have other goals that are cognitive and not necessarily practical.  In both medicine and ethics it is conceivable that cognitive goals are pursued for their own sake.[3]

     Aristotle goes on to claim that the presentation must be given in the form of an outline because "matters concerned with conduct and questions of what is good for us have no fixity."  If this remark is taken at face value, then it looks as if it clearly rules out the possibility of treating ethics scientifically since Aristotle's paradigm of science requires necessary principles that are unshakable.  But how reasonable is it to take this remark at face value?  Could Aristotle really mean that fluctuation is ever-present in matters of conduct?  It is hard to see how Aristotle could maintain this given his discussion of the human function in Nicomachean Ethics I.7, and the role that the human function plays in determining the good for human beings.  Moreover, the way Aristotle presents and characterizes the nature of eudaimonia speaks strongly against the idea that everything in matters of conduct fluctuates.  Given that the human function consists in performing activities that accord with a rational nature, there will be certain activities that are simply beneficial for human beings and others that are detrimental.  The former are to be pursued while the latter are to be avoided.[4]  In addition, how could one possibly make sense of Aristotle's unity of virtues thesis in light of the initial interpretation?

     That Aristotle could not be claiming that everything in matters of conduct lacks fixity is confirmed by the comparison between ethics and health  immediately following the remark just cited.  Although the right amount of food or drink or exercise may vary form individual to individual, there are certain activities that are conducive to health--taking in nourishment and getting exercise--and others that are clearly detrimental to health--jumping off tall buildings, drinking poison etc..[5]  So closer attention to the comparison being addressed presents a way of understanding an important part of the passage that leaves the question about ethics as an Aristotelian science open.

     The passage ends with reflections about the nature of particular cases and actions in light of the characterization of general cases and actions presented above.  Since the interpretation of the passage that attributes lack of fixity to the general cases and actions is not decisive, there is reason to be hesitant about making this application to the particular cases.  However, it is clear that Aristotle has in mind the role of phronesis (practical wisdom) near the end of the passage.  Since phronesis is directed specifically toward particular actions that appear in the minor premises of practical syllogisms, it is clear that a science of ethics would not be concerned with the role of phronesis as far as it plays this role.  Again, since Aristotelian science is not concerned with particulars and phronesis is, phronesis will fall outside the scope of a science of ethics.[6]  But is assigning a decisive role to phronesis compatible with a demonstrative paradigm of a significant portion of Aristotle’s ethics?  Even though one can interpret what Aristotle says about ethics as not ruling out a science of his ethics, it has yet to be seen whether phronesis is compatible with a demonstrative construal of Aristotle’s ethics.

For the most part relationships

      Examining for the most part relations in Aristotle is helpful for supporting the deductive construal of his ethics that I favor.  Some understand Aristotle’s phrase “hos epi to polu” in a purely statistical sense[7].  This interpretation usually  begins with the observation that there is a large subset of the class of important moral propositions that have predicates that do not always apply to their respective subjects. Since such propositions might be thought to play a significant role in moral reasoning, it is always possible that the specific circumstances that affect a particular moral decision will impede a generalization from applying correctly.  It is the ever-present possibility that such a state of affairs will arise in any particular situation that seems to show that codification of ethical principles is inappropriate.

     A technical interpretation of "for the most part" (hos epi to polu) in Aristotle that I will sketch here provides the basis for explaining how an important class of  moral principles in Aristotle's ethics can be codified in a way that supports a deductive construal of his ethics. According to the interpretation I propose, Aristotle often uses the phrase `hos epi to polu' in a technical sense that involves two component relations.  The first relation is one that holds between a subject and its capacity (dunamis) for possession of some property.  That is, Aristotle often uses the phrase ‘hos epi to polu’  to indicate that a dunamis holds of its subject necessarily.  The second relation in technical for the most part relations holds between the dunamis and its manifestation or actualization.  Because of the possibility of both internal and external impediments, and given the indeterminacy associated with matter and other factors, the actualization of any specific dunamis does not always occur.  In fact, conditions associated with matter could be such that the manifestation or actualization of necessary properties could fail to occur in a preponderance of cases.  So 'hos epi to polu' in this technical sense is not synonymous with 'most'.  It is therefore improper and misleading to understand the phrase "hos epi to polu" as meaning "most". The two expressions may sometimes overlap in important ways, but this need not always be so.  More important, many uses of the word 'most' need not imply the presence of an underlying law or relationship, but the technical sense of ‘hos epi to polu’ that Aristotle often utilizes involves some necessary law or relationship.

     Consider the for the most part proposition “Exercise for the most part produces health” that Aristotle gives at Rhetoric 1362a34.  Applying the distinction just drawn, we see the there is an important definitional relationship between the subject and predicate of this proposition.  Exercise is done for the sake of health.  One cannot fully understand what exercise is without understanding what health is.  However, it turns out that not every act involving exercise is healthy for every person.  For example, for those who are tremendously obese, running, which is one type of exercise, is not a  type of activity that would be conducive to good health.  But we would still be inclined to say that running is the type of activity that is conducive to health by nature, even though it is not actually healthy for all individuals in every case.  The reason that running  may not be healthy in some cases is because of some type of imperfection in the matter of a subject.  In the case of obesity, there is simply too much matter.  But the presence of an impediment in this case and in others does not block the causal relationship that holds between exercise and health.

     Furthermore, it could turn out that obesity is quite common, so much so that finding a person who is not obese is quite unusual.  In such a state of affairs, it would turn out that the activity of running  is not healthy for most people.  But the proposition "Exercise produces health" is nevertheless true for the most part, according to the present proposal, given the causal relationship that obtains between the subject and attribute.

     For every for the most part proposition like “Exercise produces health” there will be a corresponding relationship between the subject of the proposition and the capacity (dunamis) for the attribute in the predicate.  Furthermore, this relationship is such that the subject could not fail to possess the capacity for the attribute given the essence of the subject.  The capacity for the attribute in such relationships belongs to the subject necessarily either because it is part of the essence of the subject or because it is somehow entailed by the essence of the subject.  It is precisely because every for the most part proposition involves a necessary relationship between a subject and a dunamis that for the most part propositions fall within the domain of Aristotelian science.

 

Moral virtue and a deductive paradigm of ethics    

     How is it that the technical sense of Aristotle's phrase "for the most part" leaves room for the possibility that general moral principles could be codifiable and action guiding?  The best way to understand the matter is to start with an example of a syllogism that can be understood as beginning with determinate, specifiable premises.  Consider the reasoning that may be utilized by someone in the context of a decision about whether to return a borrowed weapon to a friend who has lost his wits:

Syllogism A

 

            Whatever is virtuous should be done.

            Repaying debts is virtuous for the most part.

            Repaying debts should be done for the most part.         

 

One could reasonably maintain that the minor premise and conclusion of this syllogism are examples of technical for the most part relations.  If they are, then the analysis of this relation I have offered would indicate that there is a necessary relation  between the subject, repayment of debt, and the capacity or dunamis for the attributes expressed in both the minor premise and conclusion respectively. Moreover, there is a necessary proposition that can be formulated about the relation between the dunamis for the predicate and its manifestation in each occurrence.  The latter follows from a strong causal principle (hereafter SCP) that Aristotle uses at Metaphysics IX., 5.  and the first necessary component relation of technical hos epi to polu relations.  SCP can be formulated in the following way:

 

Strong Causal Principle (SCP):

(E & -I & -I*) ÖR

SCP says that a natural substances realizes some capacity it has just in case impediments are absent and an appropriate efficient cause is present.[8]

     Applying these points yields this (somewhat oversimplified) analyzed version of the argument:

 

Syllogism B

 

            Whatever is virtuous should be done.

            Repaying debts will be virtuous unless impediments are present.

            Repaying debts should be done unless impediments are present.

 

The conclusion of this syllogism can then be used as a premise to generate a prescription about how to act.

 

 

 

Syllogism C

 

            Repaying debts should be done unless impediments are present.

            Impediments are not present.

            This debt should be repaid.

I think that every proposition in Syllogism A or B is codifiable.  I will argue that Syllogism C involves a codifiable first premise, a second premise based on the sensitivity that a virtuous person has, and a conclusion about an action.  If the propositions involved in Syllogism C have these characteristics, then this would provide the basis for saying that Aristotle's ethics leaves room for codifiable moral principles that are action guiding.  If a virtuous person's sensitivity does in fact play a significant role in coming to know the second premise of Syllogism C, then assigning a decisive role to virtue does not imply that a significant portion of Aristotle's ethics cannot be codified.

     Someone may reject my idea that the first premise of Syllogism C is codifiable because the unless-clause in "Repaying debts should be done unless impediments are present" is not codifiable.  I would say that the unless-clause should be understood quite generally.  Understanding this proposition involves understanding what the subject and predicate are, and that the essence of each brings with it the possibility that the predicate will not apply to the subject in every set of circumstances.  But we need not know anything about the specific circumstances in which this could happen to know that something about the essence of the subject and predicate would allow for such circumstances.  If it were necessary to know all the circumstances in which the predicate would not apply to the subject, then this proposition would not be specifiiable.   

     In claiming that the minor premise of Syllogism C is provided by a virtuous person's sensitivity I am saying that there is more than sense perception involved in the recognition of the truth of this minor premise when it is true.  Nevertheless, I am maintaining that Aristotle's ethics does allow for specifiable principles that can, together with another premise whose truth can be discovered by a virtuous person, deductively yield a conclusion about a particular action that is to be done.

      One challenge here is to explain how a necessary relation could obtain between repayment of debts and moral virtue or morally virtuous action while it would not be morally virtuous to repay a debt in certain, special circumstances.  Making explicit a necessary relationship between these concepts might help show how moral principles can have determinate, specifiable content while providing guidance about how to act in conjunction with recognition of particular facts. 

     Given that Aristotle sees all of moral virtue in reference to the end, eudaimonia, failure to repay debts must be an action that is by nature incompatible with attaining this end.  According to my understanding of Aristotle's technical sense of the phrase ‘hos epi to polu’, there is a necessary component relation holding between a subject and a capacity or disposition (dunamis).  With this point in mind, it makes sense to examine the relation between the disposition to act virtuously and disposition to repay debts. 

     What dispositions are involved in virtue?  Since justice is a virtue, it will turn out that the dispositions that come with justice will be among those that are involved in virtue.  If justice involves giving a person what is due to him or her, and repaying a debt is a case of giving to someone what is due to him or her, then virtue will involve a disposition to repay debts.  But it will not be just any disposition to repay a debt that will be included in those dispositions that come with justice.  It will at least be a disposition to repay debts because repaying a debt is a case of rendering to someone what is due to him or her, and perhaps a disposition to repay them because rendering to someone what is due to him or her is just.   

     Consider the following argument that attempts to establish the necessary connection between virtuous actions and the disposition to perform them with the disposition to repay  debts:

p1-Necessarily, if an agent has the disposition to perform virtuous acts, then such an agent has the disposition to perform just acts.

p2-Necessarily, if an agent has the disposition to perform just acts, then such an agent has the disposition to give to others what is owed to them.

p3-Necessarily, if an agent has the disposition to give to others what is owed to them, then such an agent has the disposition to repay debts.

c: Necessarily, if an agent has the disposition to perform virtuous acts, then such an agent has the disposition to repay debts.

     p1 is straightforward and easily established in view of the place of virtue within Aristotle's ethical theory. Just actions are simply a subset of virtuous actions, so it is not possible for an action to be just and not virtuous.  Hence the conditional is necessarily true.  In fact Aristotle's thesis about the unity of virtue enables us to place any virtue in the consequent of the conditional.

     p2 states a necessary truth based on the essence of justice.  Aristotle's discussion of justice in NE 5 places emphasis on the proportionality involved in justice.  It is reasonable to maintain that when someone gives a proportionate amount to another, they give what is owed to another.  The same point would apply to receiving as well.  Since there is a necessary connection between these concepts, we would expect that a necessary relation would hold between the corresponding dispositions.

     The consequent of p3, repaying debts, is simply an instance of giving what is owed.  Whoever is inclined to do the former will be inclined to do the latter.

     It is important to recognize that such a disposition or inclination need not always be acted on, since there may be some occasions when paying a debt will not be virtuous.  Indeed, those sorts of dispositions that are involved in for the most part relations will not always be acted on.  What is important here, though, is that a virtuous person will always have such a disposition.  That is all that is needed for the truth of c. 

     Since the truth of p1, p2, and p3 implies the truth of c, a strong case can be made for the idea that a disposition to repay debts is related by entailment to the disposition to perform morally virtuous actions.  It looks as though there is a necessary relation between the two notions. This necessary relation provides the basis for a determinate and specifiable for the most part relation.

     Having developed a sense according to which the recognition of certain features of a situation would constitute impediments to the manifestation of a given disposition, a person has as part of their moral character part of what developing a moral character amounts to.  Different people at different levels of virtue will have developed it differently, some well, some badly.  But it is the virtuous person who will have developed it well.  That is, virtue carries with it an appropriate sensitivity to morally relevant features such that those features whose recognition should provide impediments to the manifestation of a disposition associated with a given virtue are the features whose recognition provides such an impediment in a virtuous person.

     In the example introduced above about returning the weapon, a person who has appropriate knowledge of general ethical principles would understand that the major premise in Syllogism C is true for the most part in the technical sense.  There may be some factors or circumstances that would render repayment of some debts in some circumstances morally inadvisable.  Cases in which a potentially dangerous weapon has been borrowed from someone who has taken a mental turn for the worse since the time the weapon was borrowed would qualify.  Such a situation presents two impediments that function together--both the weapon itself and the unfortunate change of mental state.  A virtuous person who is properly aware of the relationship between these factors and what virtue requires will simply not reach a point of tension in deriving the conclusion of Syllogism C above.  Since the major premise holds for the most part and a person who is virtuous would recognize the presence of intervening factors coming to play in the second premise, such a person will recognize what action is required.  Thus, in the case just described a virtuous person will not draw the conclusion in Syllogism C, because a virtuous person would not take its minor premise to be true.  This decision is supported both by good moral sense and what the deductive paradigm of Aristotle’s ethics prescribes.  In short, the tension between what a deductive paradigm would prescribe and what good moral sense dictates in particular cases does not exist if we apply the technical interpretation of for the most part relations in Aristotle that has been developed and advocated here.

     Seeing that certain impediments are present in some situation will require full recognition of how to act were the impediments absent.  This requires a virtuous sensitivity.  Coming to know the second premise also requires knowledge of the relation between the otherwise virtuous action and the impediments that can introduce further considerations.  The virtuous sensitivity would be present here as well.  Far from diminishing the role of virtue, the account I offer requires moral virtue and leaves ample room for it.  According to the present approach, experience and perception will be required both to recognize that universal moral principles are true and to recognize how it is that impediments may affect how for the most part propositions apply in specific circumstances.

      If a person has carried out a set of deductive inferences from some small set of foundational moral principles, such deductive inference provides justification for any premise that may be used together with what a virtuous sensitivity perceives.  Since the entire set of deductive inferences, the foundational principles and the conclusions generated therefrom collectively constitute a highly specific systematic articulation of the good life, whenever appeal is made to a proposition that is part of this schema, we are doing so on the basis of our entire conception about how to live.  What distinguishes the actions of different virtuous people is not what principles they would apply in moral situations, but the sort of perception of salient features related to minor premises.  So by this way of understanding Aristotle’s ethics, a deductive paradigm is quite consistent with assigning a predominant role to phronesis.
