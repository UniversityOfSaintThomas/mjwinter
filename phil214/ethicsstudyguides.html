Introductory Ethics

Study Guide for Exam #1

This quiz will cover material beginning with Shaw and LaFollette and ending with Aquinas.  There will be one essay question to write on.  It will involve one concept with several sub-questions.  There will also be several objective questions.

You should be familiar with the different possibilities Shaw considers for a foundation for morality. Why does each fail?

What are the reasons Shaw offers for rejecting cultural relativism?

Be familiar with LaFollette's argument for moral relativism.  How does LaFollette compare language and morality?  What are the shortcomings of his argument?

Be familiar with the Mill reading. (I might provide a short passage and ask you to identify the author of it.)  Be familiar with the Mill handout.  What are some serious objections to utilitarianism?

Be familiar with the Kant reading and handout.  What are some problems with the first formulation of the CI?  Why is the second formulation not subject to these criticisms?

How does Aquinas distinguish between different kinds of law?  How is the natural law structured? In what way can the natural law change?  Is the natural law always true?  Does everyone know it? What is Aquinas' view about killing?  murder?  lying?  Why?  What would Kant and Mill have to say about these matters.
 
 

Study Guide for Exam #2
Aristotle

You should have a good handle on the "definition of happiness" diagram.

Why is happiness the highest good?- Bk. 1.  (See health analogy.) What are the characteristics of the good?  What kind of life is most supremely happy?  Why can't happiness consist solely in wealth, honor or pleasure?

Function argument- Bk. 1.  What is Aristotle's function argument?  What is the conclusion of the argument?

What is virtue?- Bk. 2.  What is the definition of virtue?  How is virtue related to happiness?  What is the mean?  Does relativity of the mean render happiness indefinite?   How does Aristotle distinguish between moral and intellectual virtues?  What intellectual virtue most properly fulfills the human function? (Bk. 6)

The nature of voluntary action- Bk.3. What is the difference between a voluntary and an involuntary action?  Why is this distinction important for the study of ethics?

Truth in action- Bk.6.  What is truth concerned with action?  Why does Aristotle distinguish between two categories of truth?  What is right desire?  What standard is used in deciding whether a desire is right?  What are some right desires?  What are some wrong desires?  Why, specifically, are such desires right or wrong?  What is Aristotle's unity of virtues thesis?
 

The problem of weakness of will- Bk. 7.  What is weakness of will?  How does Aristotle explain weakness of will?  What are the distinctions between temperance/intemperance, continence/incontinence.
