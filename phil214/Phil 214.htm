Overview

In this course we deal with some of the fundamental questions related to human conduct.  One of the most general questions is this: What type of life is best for a human being?  There are many different ways to respond to this question, and we will look at some of the most important of them.  As we shall see, some attempts are better than others. 

One of the aims of this course is to consider important aspects of some different moral theories to put us in a position to determine what a plausible moral theory might look like.  Another of our aims is to confront concrete moral questions that arise in disputes about lying, warfare, euthanasia, abortion, and other issues. 

 

In Unit 1 we examine the foundations of ethical theory.  Is it plausible to think that there are objective truths in ethics that are binding for all human beings?  Once we have dealt with that preliminary question, we look at the theories of John Stuart Mill, Immanuel Kant, and Thomas Aquinas to get some ideas about how to construct a moral theory.  We also apply some of these theories to questions about lying, stealing, killing in war, clitoridectomy, and other topics.

In Unit 2 we learn some of the most central concepts involved in Aristotle's virtue theory.  This theory is especially complex, so spelling it out requires more time and attention.  We will devote one unit of the course to that.

In Unit 3 we focus directly on applied moral issues such as social responsibility, just war, euthanasia, and abortion.
