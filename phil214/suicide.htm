Assisted Suicide and Assisted Torture

Thomas D. Sullivan

 

University of St. Thomas, St. Paul

 

March 24, 1999

 

When I was young, I worried that my Father would burn to death.  A firefighter in Chicago at a time when terrible infernos were still common, my Father had lost quite a few companions over the years.  Dad seldom talked about his job at home, and when he did he kept it light, telling tales about the antics of the boys at Truck Nine/Engine Eleven, and so I got the idea that it was best to avoid the subject of fires.  Once, though, when I was about nine or ten, I saw some very disturbing pictures in the paper.  Unable to contain myself, I blurted out “Isn’t that that an awful way to die?”  Trying to reassure me, Dad quietly replied “Those pictures suggest the people trapped in the building all burned to death, but usually the victims don’t feel the flames – they die first by asphyxiation.”  Maybe, I thought, but I wondered if he was just hoping it was true.

One thing seemed clear.  Whatever exactly “asphyxiated” meant – I was none too sure -- it was better to be asphyxiated than to go on living a few minutes in the midst of flames.  I have often since thought about the fate of Dad’s friend Dino, who wasn’t asphyxiated.  Dino was working with a team of firefighters on the top of a building, trying to open up the roof.  The building was supposed to be able to resist fire for several hours before collapsing.  But because the owner had pulled several major support beams out of the basement to make the basement store more spacious, it all collapsed in about twenty minutes.  When the roof gave way, Dino grabbed onto a cross-beam.  Firefighters, who could not immediately reach him, sprayed water on Dino from the ground below.  In agony, Dino slowly melted away.    

The thought later crossed my mind that instead of hosing Dino, somebody should have shot him.  

Crossed my mind.  Did I ever come to believe that killing an innocent person might be the right thing to do?  I don’t know.  I might have.  What I do know is that my imagination was prepared early to believe a person might be better off dead.  For that reason, I feel drawn to the case for the permissibility of euthanasia and assisted suicide. 

Drawn, but not convinced. In what follows I will try to explain why.  My focus will be personal choices, not legal and public policy issues.  Questions about decriminalization of euthanasia and assisted suicide, fair treatment of especially vulnerable patients, maintaining public trust in the medical profession, and forcing medical personnel to act contrary to conscience are all exceedingly important.   I think that Daniel Callahan and Margot White are probably right when they say “It is impossible in principle and in practice to regulate either euthanasia or physician assisted suicide successfully.”[1]  Since, however, we cannot deal with everything at once, I will here only try to draw attention to a few ideas that seem worth bearing in mind when making personal decisions about the end of life.

I will begin by attempting to get a clear view of just what is at issue.  I will then argue that if I am to ready myself to kill innocent humans, I must satisfy a burden of proof.  Next, I will consider whether the three most prominent arguments in favor of the legitimacy of euthanasia and assisted suicide provide adequate reason to believe the burden can be satisfied.

 

1.                  The Traditional Argument in Two Premises

What, if anything, is wrong with suicide, assisted suicide, and euthanasia?  The traditional short answer is that:

 

(1)   Suicide, assisted suicide, and euthanasia are all forms of intentionally killing innocent human beings

And

(2)   It is always wrong intentionally to kill innocent human beings.

 

Of course this settles nothing.  All it does is move the issue back to premise (2).  Why, after all, is it always wrong to kill human beings?  Some consequentialists have argued that it makes no more sense to say you should never kill the innocent than to say you should never leave a bucket in the hall.  Acts are judged by their consequences, not by the non-moral category to which they happen to belong. And recently, defenders of late term abortions and infanticide, such as Peter Singer, have assailed the idea that there is anything particularly special about human beings.  Moreover, it is possible to refuse to accept an absolute prohibition against killing the innocent even while recoiling from both utilitarianism and any notion that human beings are no more special than other animals.  It is necessary, therefore, to consider whether an absolute prohibition can be sustained.

To make any headway we must begin with a fairly distinct idea of just what the prohibition is.  More precisely, we must begin by stipulating which of the various meanings of the precept “You must not kill the innocent” we take to be most defensible.  We arrive at the best and most defensible understanding of the precept, I think, by dealing with four terms as follows.  

First, “human being.”  The precept takes as its domain members of the human species.  All you need to come under the protection of the precept is the attribute of being a member of the species human being.  A member of the species human being is automatically a “person” in one time-honored sense of the term, but we will avoid this term since some contemporary ethicists count as persons only those who are “well-formed“ or “have a concept of themselves as enduring subjects of experience” or “possess presently exercisable abilities.”  On the traditional understanding of the precept we are setting out, being human is enough.  It suffices.  Is it also necessary?  The precept says nothing about animals.  You can accept the precept and consistently take any position you want with respect to animal rights.

Second, “innocent.”  The qualification does not limit protection to the morally unblemished.  The idea, rather, is that if the warrant for a contemplated slaying is not to rectify or to prevent an injustice of appropriate magnitude, then the slaying is wrongful.  Furthermore, it does not automatically follow that the lives of the non-innocent may be taken.  If you accept the precept against killing innocents as absolutely binding, you still are free to take any position you want on the killing of non-innocents.  Questions about capital punishment, killing in war and killing in self-defense are distinct issues.

Third, “kill.”  The term suggests action.  But we can kill by doing nothing, that is, by refusing to act.  I need do nothing in order to starve to death an infant or helpless patient.  On the best understanding of the prohibition of killing, there is no implication that I kill only if I undertake a physical action.

Fourth and finally, “intentional.”  The qualification is meant to fix attention on those acts and omissions that have death as their aim, as opposed to acts and omissions that have death as a foreseen consequence.  As we will soon see, it is crucial to realize that the precept, as traditionally understood and restated here, does not license lethal acts provided only that the effect is foreseen but not intended.  I am certainly not innocent of the deaths of people in a building if I burn it down for the insurance money without giving a thought to the people within. 

            Why then add the qualification “intentional”?  Because acts and omissions that lead to shortening of life may be morally tolerable.  With advances in technology, life can often be preserved much longer than before but without curing an affliction.  Therapies can impose great burdens on patients or very heavily tax community resources. Treatments and (more broadly) means of preserving life that are burdensome in these ways are often called “extraordinary,” in contrast to “ordinary” means.  (Line drawing between “extra-ordinary” and “ordinary” means is often impossible, but it is equally impossible to draw lines between heavy and light loads, day and night, friend and acquaintance, rich and poor, and yet all of these distinctions are serviceable.)  Patients may sometimes properly refuse such extraordinary treatments and communities may properly refuse to supply them, particularly when the use of extraordinary means for one person involves depriving another of assistance, as in the case of organ transplants.

            The ordinary/extraordinary distinction, it should be noted, is defined in terms of  burdens and benefits.  It does not matter whether the treatment is new or old, uncommon or routine; what matter is whether or not the treatment imposes burdens disproportionate to anticipated burdens.

 

2.                  The Burden of Proof

Something identical with or close to this understanding of the prohibition of intentional killing of the innocent has found its way into the expression of religious and philosophical traditions and has been incorporated into criminal and civil law and the policy statements of medical organizations here and abroad.  As might be expected, however, stated reasons for the prohibition vary.  Some say that it is a revealed truth that human existence is a gift from God, protected by God’s own command.  Some suggest the precept is self-evident.  Some argue the precept is inferable from more evident philosophical principles.

There is much, I think, to all this.  Yet, it may be good to explore another approach one might take to the precept.  One might see the precept not to kill the innocent as binding unless a quite compelling reason can be found to make exceptions.  Good people begin with the assumption that they are not free to injure others who are doing no harm, and killing someone is harming in the extreme, since it utterly destroys the individual.  I do not need to justify greeting someone with a smile, but I certainly do need to justify plucking out his eye or flooding his body with poison.  If I ready myself to kill a human being, I assume a heavy burden of proof that the act is justifiable.

Is there a way to explain satisfactorily why killing an innocent human being might in some circumstances be permissible?

Three explanations dominate recent discussion.

 

2.                  Autonomy

The first is based on the idea of an expansive conception of autonomy. The idea is often put quite vaguely.  Geo Stone, author of a recent informative study on suicide and attempted suicide, writes “I believe that choosing if and how to end your life is your decision to make.”[2]   This sort of thing is often said, but just as often left in a state of ambiguity.  It might mean:

 

(1)   Nobody can rightly fully force you to go on living, if you do not want to.

Or it might mean:

(2)   If you kill yourself you do it rightfully, no matter when or how.  

 

Proposition (1) is certainly much more appealing than (2).  Burn victims have been literally forced to undergo excruciating treatments they should have been able to reject.  But even if (1) is true, it does not yield the conclusion that suicide may be justifiable. 

Proposition (2) has had its defenders. The Stoic philosophers talked in ways that strongly suggested killing yourself could not be wrong, no matter how or when. Often, however, both in older and more current literature, apparent endorsement of unconditional autonomy is tempered, at least tacitly, by common sense recognition that it is wrong to dispose of oneself for trifling reasons.  Thus, immediately after saying choosing if and how to end your life is your decision to make, Geo Stone stipulates, without argument, that suicide in not “indicated” unless eight conditions are satisfied.  According to Stone, it must be the case that you have “advanced terminal illness and/or your ‘quality of life’ is unacceptable,” and you must have “discussed the diagnosis, situation, and treatment choices with your doctor and [be] sure you understand them.”  On goes the list of conditions Stone would have you satisfy before you handle the business that is supposed to be yours alone.  None of this makes sense if you can’t go wrong as long as you are killing yourself.  We can wrong ourselves and wrong others by suicide. 

Stone himself lists many examples of suicides for utterly bizarre reasons, including “copycat suicides.”  On “April 8, 1986, Yukiko Okada jumped to her death from the seventh floor of her recording studio.  She had recently received an award as Japan’s best new singer.  Media attention was intense.  Thirty-three young people, one nine years old, killed themselves over the next sixteen days, twenty-one by jumping from buildings.”  Stone mentions comparable examples from around the world.  A highly publicized suicide of a Hungarian beauty queen triggered an epidemic of suicides by young women using the same method.[3]

            It seems clear enough that an appeal to unrestricted autonomy provides no satisfying explanation of why we may be free to kill or help kill innocent human beings.

 

3.         Killing and Letting Die

            A second popular justification for killing the innocent has gained prominence because with the advance of medicine we are often faced with the fact that continued treatment makes little sense even though treatment can prolong a process of dying.  With this and other situations in mind, some argue:

 

The Equivalence Argument 

(1)   It is sometimes right to refrain from giving aid with the expected result that the death of an innocent person will be hastened.

(2)   There is no significant moral difference between refraining from giving aid with the expected result that the death of an innocent person will be hastened and intentionally killing the person.  

So,

(3)   Intentionally killing a person is sometimes right.

 

Premise (1) of the Equivalence Argument is uncontroversial.  But why should anyone accept (2)?  The literature offers several arguments.

            First, some claim, there is no legitimate contrast to be drawn between acting with the intention to bring about a state of affairs and merely foreseeing that your action will eventuate in some state of affairs.  If you can foresee that your action will have a certain consequence, then you intend the consequence.  You cannot maintain that you only foresee and not intend what you know will be a consequence of your action. With his customary precision, Roderick Chisholm articulates the equation of the foreseen and intended in what he calls “The Diffusiveness of Intention Principle”:[4]

 

If a rational man acts with the intention of bringing about a certain state of affairs P, and if he believes that by bringing about P he will bring about the conjunctive state of affairs P and Q, then he does act to bring about the conjunctive state of affairs P and Q.

 

The Diffusiveness of Intention Principle seems plausible.  If you hammer a nail hard with the expectation that by so doing you will drive the nail into the wallboard, then you intend to hammer the nail and drive it into the wall.  And, similarly, it might seem, if you give a patient a pain-killer that will foreseeably shorten life, you must intend both to kill pain and kill the one in pain.

            But as plausible as the Diffusiveness of Intention Principle may seem, the  principle turns out to be false.  Consider Judith.  She stutters.  Still, she is determined to make a good speech in defense of her Mother’s reputation.  Since Judith has stuttered all of her life, she believes she will stutter.  Given the Diffusiveness principle, she intends to make a good speech and to stutter her way through it.  That is odd enough as it stands, since the stuttering detracts from the speech.  But in addition, given that she tries not to stutter, and given that trying to bring about the state of affairs not-s entails intending to bring about the state of affairs not-s, Judith must also intend that she not stutter.  Putting it all together, if the Diffusiveness of Intention Principle is correct, Judith must intend to make a good speech, stutter, and not-stutter. 

Or again, Judith is trying for a new world record in the high jump.  She believes she will fail; everyone else has.  Still, a world class jumper, she has a chance.  She exerts herself with all her strength.  Her intention, of course, is certainly not to knock the bar down – she’s trying her best to clear it.  But the Diffusiveness Principle informs us she also intends to knock it down, since she believes that she will.

Would it help to replace “believe” with “know?”  Does the Diffusiveness Principle hold if we know the unwanted result will occur?  No, it doesn’t, because even then we get the same results.  I limp.  Trying to get out of the way of an onrushing train, I know I will limp, but I still run as fast as I can.  Given the Diffusiveness Principle, I intend to run as fast as I can, limp, and not-limp.  This cannot be right.  In general, when an anticipated effect of an action is not only unwanted but inconsistent with what one is trying to achieve, then it is highly implausible to argue that contradictions exist in the individual’s intentions.[5]  

So premise (2) of the Equivalence argument cannot be established by citing the Diffussiveness of Intention Principle. Premise (2), however, can be argued in a different way.  In his often anthologized, widely influential essay “Active and Passive Euthanasia,” James Rachels argues that the traditional distinction between active and passive euthanasia is incoherent.  Rachels’s essay attacks a statement by the American Medical Association representative of the traditional idea that intentionally terminating the lives of patients is wrong, but withholding of extraordinary means of treatment sometimes right.  Rachels contends that on the traditional view “passive euthanasia” is permissible.  But this means that parents and doctors who refuse to correct the bowel obstruction of a Downs Syndrome child are morally in the clear; they can stand by and watch their child wither from dehydration and die because they aren’t doing anything, and so are not doing anything wrong.  But they would be doing something wrong if they gave the baby a relatively painless lethal injection.  Patently absurd, Rachels argues.  And he is right. 

Partially.  It would be absurd to maintain that you are morally in the clear as long as you do not do anything.  But no competent defender of the tradition ever said you were.  You have to use ordinary means when they are available.  Since the surgery imposes no disproportionate burdens on anyone, standing by and refusing to do anything means failing to use ordinary means.  Though citing a statement sanctioning only the withholding of extraordinary means, Rachels ignores that distinction and substitutes one nobody had made, a distinction between acting and standing by, as if what counted was whether you move a part of your body.  Since the tradition had endlessly insisted that there were sins of omission as well as sins of commission, Rachels’ attack failed to make cognitive contact with the alleged target of the attack.  As Bonnie Steinbock has observed, so did other noted attacks on the position.[6]

Could premise (2) of The Equivalence Argument be shown to be true?  Well, maybe, but to demonstrate the moral equivalence of withholding aid and intentionally killing in all cases one must do better than show the equivalence of the two in cases where ordinary means are withheld.  Focusing on cases where ordinary means are withheld,  Rachels could show that there is indeed no moral difference between an uncle’s drowning a young nephew in a bathtub in order to get an inheritance and letting the child drown if he finds him face down in a tub after falling.  But this and similar comparisons fail to establish the general moral equivalence between withholding aid and intentionally killing; to do that the equivalence must be established when the means withheld are extraordinary.  This seems exceedingly difficult to show.  Does anyone think that because more people need organ transplants than we can now provide, we are therefore free to terminate the lives of those who fall below low on the recipient’s list?

The Equivalence Argument might be pressed further, but let us move on to consider a far more formidable objection to the traditional position.

 

4.         Mercy

The strongest objection to the prohibition of killing the innocent takes as its point of departure the awful realities some confront.  It is extremely tempting to accept this line of reasoning.

 

The Argument From Mercy

(1)              In some circumstances an individual’s suffering may be so great non-existence would be preferable to existence.[7]

(2)              If (1), then suicide can be rational and morally permissible.

(3)              And if suicide can be rational and morally permissible, assisted suicide and euthanasia can as well.

From which it follows that:

(4)              Suicide, assisted suicide, and euthanasia can be morally permissible.

 

I have already expressed considerably sympathy for premise (1) of this Argument from Mercy.  There are a thousand varieties of grief so great we find ourselves saying, “She would be better off dead.”  Opponents of mercy killing often strenuously reject this idea.[8]  Some point to a logical problem.  How could Judith be better of dead if Judith no longer exists?  Others rightly observe that in some mouths the phrase “better off dead” betrays a contempt for persons whose afflictions are seen as somehow diminishing their value as persons.[9]  Others rightly observe that it is relatively rare that nothing whatever can be done for someone – burning to death is atypical – and that science and medicine offer far more by way of palliation than even some professionals might imagine.[10]  It can also be pointed out that some terribly afflicted people heroically endure their suffering with amazing joy. 

But does any or all of this effectively dispel the idea that one might be better off dead?   I’m afraid I do not see how.  It does seem quite possible that life in certain circumstances would not be choice-worthy.  If all that is under consideration is my own condition, and not what I might owe someone in justice, then, it seems, it would be rationally preferable not to exist than to exist.[11]  Would anybody want to live ten minutes longer if the ten minutes were to be spent amidst flames?  Why do firefighters hope that if they die in a fire it will be by asphyxiation, not by more slowly roasting to death?  And why is it sometimes so reassuring to be told that an accident victim “died instantly”?

Some will object that by saying that I have as much as assumed that there is no providential God. If we believe in a revelation of a providential God, we are entitled to believe that our lives at every moment retain significance, and perhaps the greatest significance in the most trying circumstances.[12]   We are entitled to hope what Socrates dared not hope.  As portrayed in the Phaedo, Socrates believed that he would be better off dead, since his soul would then be free of the body weighing it down.  Nonetheless, since he was the property of superior beings, he was not free to terminate his earthly life.  Though Socrates said nothing whatever about the gods’ love for human beings, he believed the gods owned him, and hence suicide was wrong.  We may dare hope more than Socrates hoped.  We may hope that a loving God is closer to none more than to the most sorely afflicted, and that there is no grief so great that God cannot or will not draw a greater good out if it.  We may dare to hope all this – I do.  But many do not.  And we do well to consider where the argument goes if we do not permit ourselves to invoke any belief about the providence of God.

Let us assume, then, at least for the sake of our exploratory discussion, that premise (1) is true.  Let us for the sake of discussion assume that either God does not exist or does not intervene to make life in extreme conditions worth living. 

What then?  Well, we still have premises (2) and (3) to consider.  Premise (2) tells us that if in some circumstances existence is not choice-worthy, then suicide is rational and morally permissible.  This may be doubted on the grounds that killing yourself might involve doing an injustice to someone who needs you.   But often self-destruction does no one else an injustice.  Friends, family, professional care-givers may wish that flourishing life could be restored, and they may miss you, but they may also realize that baring a miracle, a cure is out of the question.  In these circumstances, they may prefer that the end come quickly and see in the event no loss to themselves that could be properly depicted as a loss of something due them.  Then too there is the infinitely sad case of the solitary individual whose death by natural causes or by self-destruction is mourned by no one.

Realizing this possibility, opponents of euthanasia often protest against (2).  In language reminiscent of Aquinas and Kant, they urge that even if non-existence should be preferable to existence you still would not be entitled to kill yourself.  A reason ascribed to Aquinas is that basic goods cannot be attacked, even to achieve a good end, and life is a basic good.[13]  And Kant argues that

 

Man cannot renounce his personality so long as he is a subject of duty, hence so long as he lives….  To destroy the subject of morality in one’s own person is to root out the existence of morality itself from the world; and yet morality is an end in itself.  Consequently, to dispose of oneself as a mere means to an arbitrary end is to abase humanity in one’s person.[14]

 

There is, perhaps, a great deal to these protests, but instead of trying to see how they might be directly supported, let us instead try a different tack.  Let us consider how (2) and (3) might be further generalized.

As it stands, premise (2) allows suicide is rational and morally permissible on the condition, stipulated in (1), that an individual’s suffering is so great non-existence would be preferable to existence.  But (2) does not tell us suicide is morally permissible only if an individual’s suffering is very great.  Nor should it tell us that.  For there is no good reason why people must actually suffer a great deal before they have a right to kill themselves.  The mere anticipation should be enough.  If I am a genetic time-bomb, why must I wait until circumstances are desperate before I make my exit?  If you can jump to your death, do you have to wait until you have first been all but consumed by encircling flames?

And, to carry the reasoning forward another step, given that I may commit suicide to avoid great personal suffering, it would certainly seem that I could also extinguish my life in order to prevent great suffering by others.  People have killed themselves in order to leave food for other members of a stranded group.  Nothing seems to stand in the way, as long as suicide is permissible to avoid suffering.  The suffering of others, especially many others, is at least as significant as my own suffering.  Besides, it can become a component of my own suffering -- or yours.

Imagine the following situation.  Terrorists bent on revenging a grievance against Arthur and his wife kidnap one of their children and threaten to torture the child unless Arthur sends them one of his own eyes.  His wife, a surgeon, must do the deed.  The terrorists want a video tape of it all.  Well, why not?  People donate organs for good causes.

Make it both eyes.  Still ok?  Two eyes to rescue a child from torture? 

All the organs, one by one. 

No, all at once.  Arthur is to seat himself in a chair; his wife is to douse him with gasoline, and then…. Why shouldn’t they consent? 

Not enough at stake?  Raise the number of children to four and add three friends.  A whole village, a city, a nation.  Why not comply?

It might be argued that no matter what the stakes, Arthur and his wife should not accede to the demand.  Arthur is a human being.  A child of God.  Of course this answer will not do.  God is out of the picture. 

But he cannot rationally destroy himself or parts of himself for the sake of others.  By why not, exactly?  Why not at least parts?  How is it that he can destroy his entire self to avoid his own pain, but not even a fingertip to rescue from excruciating pain everybody on a continent? 

No, the couple should refuse, one might argue, because they have no reason to believe the promises of the terrorists.  If the terrorists will kill, they will lie….  Maybe.  These terrorists, however, take oaths very gravely, and they have a sterling record of keeping their word.  They release captives if their conditions are met, and if they are not, they do exactly what they threaten.

No, the couple should refuse because what is demanded is just too awful.  Is it?  Buddhist monks and others set themselves ablaze in order to protest the Vietnam war.  Mothers and fathers and firefighters have run into flames to rescue children.  Having allowed that people may kill themselves for a good cause, how are we supposed deny them the only means they have to reach the end? 

The Mercy Argument is moving, almost irresistible.  Yet, we should resist.  For if we accept (2), if we allow accept the notion that an individual’s suffering may make suicide rational and morally permissible, it is very difficult to see what good reason we could have for rejecting an number of related propositions:

 

(2)*              Suicide is morally permissible even when you are flourishing.

And so too:

(2)**    Suicide by awful means of self-destruction is morally permissible for the sake of others.

 

And given these variations on (2), variations on (3) also seem eminently reasonable, variations that lead to others torturing you to death.

            Do such considerations compellingly show that suicide, assisted suicide and euthanasia are always impermissible?  No.  Perhaps there is some fault in the way the consequences have been spelled out.  Perhaps the consequences are acceptable.  “Very well,” one might say, “it had not occurred to me that these might be reasonable consequences of giving up on the precept ‘Never intentionally kill the innocent,’ but I am ready to believe it may be right and just to torture innocents to death.”  Yet, we must recall our primary purpose.  We have set out to find a justification for killing the innocent that leaves us with little doubt that we are indeed morally in the clear if we decide to kill in certain circumstances.  If the rationale we propose has the consequence that assisted torture is every bit as justified as assisted suicide, we do not seem to be yet in possession of an argument that satisfies the burden of proof. 

What all of this may well show is that philosophy can take us only so far.  Theologians have argued that divine revelation must steady our perception of the foundations of morality.  It may be – and thank God for it – that our only hope is to accept the full revelation of the Word that proclaims:  “I came that they may have life, and have it abundantly" (John 10:10).
