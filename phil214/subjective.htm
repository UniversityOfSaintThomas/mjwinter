Refutation of Subjectivism
M. Winter

Imagine a conversation between A and B.  A is subjectivist.  B has more ordinary beliefs about morality.  This short dialogue below indicates that a person with ordinary beliefs about morality can refute the subjectivist in short order by asking the right question.
 

A: "There are no objective moral standards."

-Since this proposition has no prescriptive element, it is not a moral claim. So it is not inconsistent as it stands.
-One can reasonably say that a standard is objective when it can be applied to more than one individual and when it holds true independently of an individual's beliefs and desires.

B: "Since I naively believe that are at least some objective moral standards, but your position implies that I am mistaken, could you please tell me how I should conduct myself?"

-B is justified in asking this question since there are presumptive grounds for thinking that there at least some objective moral standards, e.g., one should not torture infants for pleasant amusement. The burden of proof rests on the shoulders of someone who would deny that there are objective moral standards since experience speaks in favor of at least some. Also, the claim "No A is B" is quite strong.
-Since A offers universal claims about the nature of morality, B is justified in asking A how morality applies to him/her.

A: "Do whatever you feel like doing."or
    "Pursue any desire you might have." or
    "Decide for yourself."

-An advocate of subjectivism is likely to make one of these claims or some other variation of some such claim.
-All of the above are moral claims because each contains a prescriptive element. Moreover, each proposition provides an objective moral standard because one can praise or blame another by appealing to any of these propositions. Each of the propositions contains subjective content, but they are all objective claims nevertheless. So moral relativism is incoherent if it is relevant since any response like this from A is incompatible with A's first statement.
-If A refuses to make an assertion at this stage, then his position is uninteresting. If A cannot make a meaningful claim beyond his first assertion, then he really cannot pretend to understand what morality is about-- in this case A's position is not intellectually compelling.
-So subjectivism is either incoherent or not intellectually compelling.
