Sample Analysis:

Here is an example of how to construct and critique an argument from a short article.  In the section “The nature of language”in Hugh  LaFollette's article (http://www.stpt.usf.edu/hhl/papers/relative.htm) we might construct the following argument.

 

 

Language is not fixed by rules.

Morality is like language.

Morality is not fixed by rules.

 

LaFollette begins by making a case for the first premise by claiming that since the purpose of language is effective communication, and since there will be many situations in which ignoring or breaking the rules of language is the best way to communicate effectively, language cannot be fixed by rules.  He says “Rules of grammar and style not only fail to distinguish minimally acceptable prose from masterful style, occasionally they offer direction we should ignore.” He then quotes Thomas Paine to show how a phrase that does not conform to standard linguistic usage can convey an idea very well.  LoFollette supports the second premise, which is not explicitly stated, by comparing language to morality.  Just as the purpose of language is effective communication, he seems to think the purpose of morality is to get along.  Language and morality seem to share many traits.

Although this argument by analogy is correct in its form, we might question the truth of the premises. Regarding the first premise, it is not clear that language is not fixed by rules.  In fact, there is a lot of work from linguists indicating that there are common features of all languages.  These features are very general, but they are uniform.  This suggests that at some level language is fixed by rules.  Noam Chomsky speaks of a universal grammar as capturing some of these commonalities.  This universal grammar might spring from a common human nature.   For example, when I tell you my house is brown, you assume I am talking about the exterior.  We all assume that.  It seems to be innate.  If all humans share a common nature, then we might appeal to aspects of our nature to ground morality, which would run contrary to the relativism LaFollette favors.

The second premise makes a comparison between language and morality, so it rests on an analogy.  To undercut an analogy, one must find some characteristic rendering language and morality dissimilar.   Morality is concerned with action while language is based on communication.  Why should we think that the two overlap so easily given that they are concerned with quite different domains?  Absent some good reasons for thinking they are similar, I think we should be reluctant to accept the analogy.

     

Since the first premise of the argument seems to be undercut by strong evidence from the field of linguistics, the main argument LaFollette offers does not appear to be sound.
