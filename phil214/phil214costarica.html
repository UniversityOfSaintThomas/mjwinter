Introduction

In this course we grapple with some fundamental questions related to human conduct.  Since all human beings share a common nature, it seems appropriate to wonder how human beings should conduct themselves if they wish to flourish.   What, after all, is human flourishing?  Is there only one way a human being can flourish?  Is there a notion of human flourishing that can exist beyond cultural bounds?  Could a human being flourish in dire poverty or in the grip of a terrible disease? 

Great minds in the Western tradition have made important contributions to thinking about human conduct, and their insights can help us deal with these kinds of questions.  This course will build on these contributions, with an emphasis on both theoretical and applied ethics.  In the area of ethical theory, we focus the contributions from Aristotle, St. Thomas Aquinas, John Stuart Mill, Immanuel Kant, and  Carlos Eduardo Maldonado.   Examining the work of these authors helps us get a better handle on concepts such as human flourishing, human dignity, moral virtue, and the common good.  Once we have a well grounded understanding of these ideas, we can see how they play out in different aspects of contemporary life. 

Our privilege to study this material in Costa Rica gives us the opportunity to see how some important ethical problems arise in a Latin American culture.   We will reflect on issues in applied ethics that are special to this region including the rights of workers, the implications of trade agreements with the United States and other nations, the economic and environmental impact of tourism, the ecomomics and ethical aspects of coffee production, and how a nation can survive in the modern world without a military.
