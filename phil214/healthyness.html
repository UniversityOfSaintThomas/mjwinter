    Understanding Aristotle's Ethics: Happiness and Health

    I think it is helpful to try to understand Aristotle's concept of happiness as it compares to healthiness.  Aristotle points to a parallel between ethics and medicine, so it might prove useful to consider some of the features that these domains share.

    The goal of medicine is healthiness.  Healthiness is not a concept that we construct for our own convenience, but something that results when the human body is functioning as it should.  To become healthy we must learn about the kinds of things that constitute healthiness for human beings.  We discover that some activities are always unhealthy, e.g., drinking poison, swimming in freezing water, etc..  Any amount of these activities is unhealthy.  On the other hand, there are some activities that we must engage in to be healthy, e.g., eating healthy food, getting exercise, rest, etc..  But too much of any of these things can become bad.  Too much exercise is hard on the body, for example.  So engaging in the right kinds of activities is not enough, we must aim for the right amount.

    We can see how these points are parallel to ethics.  Happiness is an activity that follows from our function as human beings.  We do not decide what our function will be-- it is given to us by nature.  In order to be happy we need to understand what our nature is.  Once we have this understanding, we learn that some activities are always bad to do, e.g., theft, adultery and murder.  On the other hand, some activities are necessary for our fulfillment, e.g., seeking knowledge, making friends, etc..   We must choose the right amount of each of  these activities.  We will not be happy if we lock ourselves in a room to read all the time.  We are social animals.  We should seek the mean in all that we do.

    Just as being healthy depends on good fortune, so does happiness.  If one eats a balanced diet and exercises regularly but develops a disease from drinking contaminated food, then one will not be healthy.  The same point applies in the moral case.  Fulfillment comes from being virtuous in favorable circumstances.  It is hard to see how someone could be happy if he/she is virtuous but subject to torture and other misfortunes.

    Leading a healthy lifestyle is a habit just as leading a virtuous life is.  Neither is necessarily pleasant at first, but both are seen as being pleasant the more they are done.  It is difficult to change from an unhealthy lifestyle to a healthy one.  It is at least as difficult to abandon vicious habits for virtuous ones.  We bestow higher praise on those who do the latter because the journey is longer.

    Just as we can know what the right foods are and fail to eat them, we can know what the right activities but fail to do them.  This is weakness of will.  This phenomenon is as prevalent in health as it is in ethics.
