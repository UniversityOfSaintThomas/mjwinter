The Basics of Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism has been quite influential primarily because of its fundamental simplicity and its ability to explain a significant range of human choices.  At the basis of this theory is the "Greatest Happiness Principle" that states:

 Actions are right in proportion to their promotion of happiness, and wrong in proportion to their promotion of unhappiness.

Happiness amounts to pleasure for Mill, who draws a distinction between the quantity and the quality of pleasures.  Humans are capable of pleasures higher in quality than those a beast is capable of enjoying.  Intellectual pleasures are probably highest on the quality scale while "pleasures of the flesh" are at the bottom.  So if the sort of pleasure utilitarians consider is restricted to the quantity of pleasures as some have thought, then it does seem to be a doctrine worthy of swine.  If, however, one is willing to acknowledge that some pleasures are simply better than others, then it is not so clear that this objection will stand.

So how does one apply the Greatest Happiness Principle in particular cases?  Since we ultimately want to decide which action among some set of alternatives yields the most pleasure, it is helpful to assign a standard of measurement: "hedons".  A hedon is just a unit of pleasure.  So the best action is the one with the best sum total of hedons.

Here are the steps involved in applying the Greatest Happiness principle:
(1) Determine all alternative actions that could be performed in some situation.
(2) Assign the appropriate amount of hedons to each alternative action.
(3) Add up the hedons to see which action is best.

Act Utilitarianism focuses on the net utility of any particular action.  The action with the greatest number of hedons in comparison with others is the one that ought to be performed.

Rule Utilitarianism focuses on the net utility of following certain rules.  One must ask him/herself which rule would yield the best overall consequences if it were followed.

Both versions of utilitarianism are consequentialist in nature, i.e. the consequences of actions are of primary moral relevance.  Intention, duty, obligation, equity, justice and rights are all of (at best) secondary relevance.

 

 

Problems with Utilitarianism/Consequentialism:

-When considering the quantity of pleasure (hedons), we must account for both the greater amount of pleasure and the greater number of people.  Things begin to get more complicated.  So the theory is not as simple as it appears initially.

-The theory takes no account of equity and justice, both of which seem to be important.
Person         Hedons (Case 1)   Hedons (Case 2)
  a                     5                                             4
  b                     5                                 4
  c                     5                                 3
  d                     1                                 3
  e                     1                                 3

Case 2 is the more just and equitable, but there is nothing in utilitarianism which would suggest that one should opt for Case 2.

-The theory does not take into account personal rights, and thereby abandons the idea of human dignity-- that all humans are ends in themselves and should never be treated as means to ends.

-Consequentialism leaves open the idea that the ends can justify the means.  Reflecting on a few cases shows that this principle is unacceptable.
