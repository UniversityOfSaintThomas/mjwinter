October 9, 2006

Handout Regarding the Question and Core Definitions and Argument

Tom Sullivan

Draft 1 Oct 3 2006

 

1.         The Question: Can a purely secular case be made out for fundamental absolute rights?  [More exactly: Do we by nature alone, i.e., excluding the divine, have fundamental absolute right?]

 

2.         Definitions:

 

A human being enjoys an absolute human right if and only if there is some kind of action (or omission) A such that it is always wrong intentionally to do A to any human or always wrong intentionally to refrain from doing A for any human, unless extraordinary means are required.  (For example, if it is always wrong to lie to any human being, then every human would have an absolute right not to be lied to.  And, if it is always wrong not to provide food to the starving when it is readily available, then every human would have an absolute right to be fed.)

 

A human being enjoys a fundamental  absolute right if and only if it is always wrong to assault the innocent or to fail to provide what is necessary for life or liberty when ordinary (= proportionate) means for doing so are available. (Here “assault” will mean an attempt to damage someone physically against that person’s will at the time.)

 

2.         Positions

 

My positions expressed at greater length in The Agnositic Inquirer, a forthcoming work co-authored with Dr. Sandra Menssen. I should note that while the substance of the position was thought through together, since the arrangement for this debate was set a few months ago, I’ve worked on the topic alone.  Dr. Menssen should not be held responsible for any unintentional deviations on my part from the view we expressed in The Agnositic Inquirer. 

 

I believe we have fundamental absolute rights, but that we do so in virtue of the way God has ordered reality, including life beyond this life.  I believe the natural law is, as Aquinas said, a participation in the eternal law, as Aquinas said, and as popes have said as well.  Professor Winter concurs in the judgment that there are fundamental absolute rights, but calls into question the notion that we have them solely in virtue of a divine ordination of natural and supernatural reality.  I too call my own position into question, inasmuch as, these are difficult matters, and I don’ pretend to have a knock-down argument for my position.  But tonight it is my job to make out a case for my position, and Professor’s Winter’s job to attack it.  And so I will do my best to make my reasons clear in the allotted time.

 

4.         A word about the position I am going to oppose, the notion that we have fundamental absolute rights naturally.  

 

This has great personal pull.  As a philosopher I’d like to have a purely rational argument for fundamental absolute rights.  How else can we go into the public square and argue with others who do not share our religious views?  [I won’t try to answer that question during the presentation, but will speak to it during the discussion following the presentations if anyone is interested in the issue.]

 

Furthermore, I as a philosopher, I am drawn to the kind of things that are said about natural law and what is said in the Kantian tradition about the dignity of the human person.  And as an American I am also taken with sonorous proclamation in American Scripture, the Declaration of Independence: that the existence of inalienable rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness is “self-evident.”

 

5.         Arguments for the negative

 

5.A      Some considerations that moved me in the direction of thinking the ground must be in the divine order of things, before thinking of the suicide argument.

 

Argument 1.     The existence of the right, pace Jefferson and others is not self-evident.  Besides, Jefferson brings in a creator.

 

Argument 2.     Just as removing axioms from a mathematical system will curtain what can be derived – think of neutral geometry – so removal of foundation ideas of theism and Christianity should be expected to limit the moral “theorems” that can be derived.

 

Argument 3      Philosophers who support absolute rights are almost always either believers.

 

Argument 4      Though it is commonly assumed the Christian tradition is four-square behind the notion that fundamental absolute rights are grounded in nature, the tradition is anything but crystal clear on the point. 

 

Think about Aquinas’s definition of natural law as a participation in the eternal law, and about the remarks by him and others on exemptions from the commandments on the second tablet.

 

Also see Aquinas on the foundation of the old law.

 

See Aquinas and Scotus on dispensations from the second tablet, prompted by reflection on the alleged immortalities” of the patriarchs. 

 

Though the Church sometimes uses Kantian language about treating persons as ends, never solely as means – John Paul II was found of this expression -- reference by the church to human dignity is almost never in a purely Kantian spirit; reference is almost always made to the “image of God” and to divine providence.

 

Argument 5      It is not easy to find a purely secular argument that establishes the positive conclusion.  (Ask yourself about your favorite, “Just how does it go, from top to bottom?)  This is the thought that plagued me.  I couldn’t set out the argument in satisfactory form, a form that blunted consequentialist imaginings about catastrophes.   Nor could I with Alan Donagan dismiss such scenarios as “absurd fantasies.” Not in this age of relentless terrorism, missing atomic weapons, and anthrax.

 

Argument 6      The main defenses – neo-Thomistic and Kantian -- ruled out suicide.  And this has fundamental consequences for Kantian and Thomistic arguments regarding absolute prohibitions and fundamental rights. This thought is elaborated in “The Main Argument” immediately following.


 

 

 

The Main Argument: The Suicide Argument

 

1.         If we have fundamental, absolute human rights, then either we have such rights naturally or supernaturally.  (We have them naturally if we have them whether God exists or not; we have them supernaturally only if God exists.)

 

2.         We do not have fundamental absolute rights naturally.

 

3.         So, either we do not have fundamental, absolute rights or we do so supernaturally. 

 

Premise 1  With a bit of explanation of the terms, premise 1 seems obvious enough.

 

Premise 2. This requires argument of course.  So, here it is in outline.

 

2.1.      We have fundamental absolute rights naturally only if it is necessarily impermissible to assault innocents against their will.   [Definition of “fundamental absolute right.]

 

2.2       There are times when, leaving God out of the picture, nonexistence is rationally preferable to existence.  (We can use the phrase “better off dead”, provided we mean what was just said about rational preference.)  [Assumption]

 

2.3       If there are times when, leaving God out of the picture, nonexistence rationally preferable to existence, suicide (that is, killing one’s innocent self) is morally permissible.  [Assumption]

 

2.4       If suicide is morally permissible, then it is not necessarily impermissible to kill and innocent person. 

 

2.5       If it is not is not necessarily impermissible to kill and innocent person., then it is not necessarily impermissible to kill an innocent person when it is against that person’s will.  [Assumption]

 

2.6       So, it is not necessarily impermissible to kill an innocent person when it is against that person’s will. [Assumption]

 

2.7       Therefore, we do not have fundamental absolute rights naturally.  [Standard logic]

 

 

 

6.         Close

 

So goes the outline of the case for the idea that God is the foundation of fundamental human rights, just as Jefferson hinted.   Were I delivering a lecture, I should add objections and responses.  But happily I can leave the objections to my very able colleague and to member of the audience.
