Introduction

In this course we will grapple with some of the fundamental questions related to human conduct.  In general, since all human beings share certain important characteristics, we want to be in a position to intelligently think about how we should conduct ourselves.  There are many different ways that we could respond to this question-- we will look at some of the most important approaches.  We will see that some of them are better than others. 

One of the aims of this course is to consider which moral theory is most adequate.  Another aim is to consider some concrete moral questions such as: Are we ever morally justified in taking the life of an innocent human being?  What if the consequences of taking a life are beneficial?  How about if the person is suffering gravely?  Must we always tell the truth? 

In Unit 1 we will examine the foundations of ethical theory.  Is it plausible to think that there are objective truths in ethics that are binding for all human beings?  Once we have dealt with that question, we will look at the theories of John Stuart Mill, Immanuel Kant, and Thomas Aquinas to get some ideas about how to construct a moral theory.  We also apply some of these theories to questions about lying, stealing, killing in war, clitoridectomy, and other topics.

In Unit 2 we learn some of the most central concepts involved in Aristotle's virtue theory.  This theory is complex, so spelling it out requires more time and attention.

In Unit 3 we focus directly on issues such as social responsibility, just war, euthanasia, and abortion.
