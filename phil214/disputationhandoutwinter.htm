Disputation: Are moral absolutes possible without God?

 

Michael Winter

Department of Philosophy

University of St. Thomas

 

Preliminary point:  We agree that moral absolutes are not possible without God in the sense that God is the author of our nature.  If there is no God, there is no human nature.  If there is no human nature, then there are no absolute prohibitions or fundamental absolute rights based on that nature.  The question at issue tonight is whether we need to appeal to God’s word to justify fundamental absolute rights.  Can fundamental absolute rights be know and justified on the basis of human reason without the assistance of God’s word?

 

Focal premise from Dr. Sullivan’s sub-argument about suicide:

 

2.5 If it is not necessarily impermissible to kill an innocent person, then it is not necessarily impermissible to kill an innocent person when it is against that person’s will.

 

What needs to be shown:  Establishing that 2.5 is false requires showing that the antecedent of the conditional could be true while the consequent is false.   Showing that it is impermissible to kill an innocent person when it is against that person’s will even if we concede that it is not necessarily impermissible to kill an innocent person would be sufficient to stop the argument from going through.  

 

How to show it: There are countless examples of situations in which we find pairs of propositions that hold for the most part whose intersection generates a much stronger principle. 

 

Consider these examples:

            A1) Don’t take large doses of sleeping pills.

                A2) Don’t drink large quantities of alcohol.

a) Never take large doses of sleeping pills while drinking lots of alcohol.

Or

            B1) Don’t inhale ammonia vapors.

                B2) Don’t inhale bleach vapors.

b) Never inhale ammonia vapors mixed with bleach vapors.

 

Using these examples as a model, we can look at the following pair of fundamental propositions:

 

C1) It is morally wrong to act against basic human goods.

C2) It is morally wrong to seriously violate agent autonomy.

 

Rape and homicide violate (C1) and (C2), and this probably why we find widespread consensus that these acts are always morally objectionable.  But suicide does not clearly violate (C2), and so we should expect that case for its prohibition would be more difficult to make.  But the overall argument for fundamental absolute rights should not rest on the case for an absolute prohibition of suicide.
