Does a Pregnant Woman Have Four Eyes?
Thomas J. Sullivan

Grey  As far as I'm concerned a woman has the right to terminate her pregnancy at any time, in any way, for any reason she wishes.

Black  Why?

Grey   Because it is her body, and therefore strictly a personal matter. I take my stand with John Stuart Mill.  A person should be free to do as he or she sees fit in private matters.  And there is nothing more private than what to do with your own body.  I don't see how anyone could refute this argument.

Black  It's difficult to refute, all right.

Grey  I'm surprised to hear you say that.  You mean you're coming around to my point of view on abortion?

Black  No, of course not.  I wish I could find a gentler way to put it, but the reason I think your position is hard to argue against is because it's so weak that it's difficult to make the absurdity more obvious than it already is.

Grey  What!  You think Mill's privacy principle is absurd?  You think the state does have a right to move in anywhere it pleases and interfere with strictly personal choices?

Black.  No.  It's not the principle, but the application that is silly.  Libertarians on abortion like to cite Mill's principle, but none of them ever seem to have read the last chapter in On Liberty, with its cautions about applying the principle properly (reaching for her portable Mill, she reads):
 

A person should be free to do as he likes in his own concerns, but he ought not to be free to do as he likes in acting for another, under the pretext that the affairs of the other are his own affairs.  The state, while it respects the liberty of each in what specially regards himself, is bound to maintain a vigilant control over his exercise of a power which allows him to possess over others.


And do you know where Mill thinks the state often fails to provide adequate protection for the party abused in the name of privacy?
 

It is in the case of children, that misapplied notions of liberty are a real obstacle to the fulfillment by the state of its duties.  One almost thinks that man's children were supposed to be literally, and not metaphorically, a part of himself.


Grey  Wait a minute!  Of course the child isn't literally a part of its father.  And, after birth, it isn't even literally part of its mother.  But we're talking about a fetus inside the mother's uterus wholly dependent on her for nourishment, oxygen, and life itself.  And so it's a part of mother.

Black  Literally?

Grey  Yes, literally.

Black  Would you say this about a fetus that was a few weeks along, and had eyes and hands, a sense of touch on the mouth, and so forth?

Grey  Yes, I don't see that this detracts one bit from the fact that it's attached and dependent, and therefore literally a part.

Black  Well, a patient on a kidney machine is attached and dependent, but, I take it, not part of machine.  But let that pass.  Tell me, do you think that if A is a part of B and B is a part of C, that A is a part of C?

Grey  I thought we were talking about abortion.  That sounds like a riddle.

Black  It's not.  Let me explain little.  That brick over there is part of the wall, and the wall itself a part of the building.  And so the brick is part of building, right?

Grey   Of course.  So what?

Black  And the finger is part of the hand, and since the hand is part of the body, the fingers are part of body.

Grey  Yes, but I really don't know where we're going with this.

Black  Bear with me a bit longer.  It's obvious, then, that if A is a part of B and B is a part of C, then A is a part of C.  The part/whole relation is transitive, as logicians say.

Gray  Yes.

Black  How many eyes does a well formed fetus have?

Grey  Two.

Black  Its mother?

Grey.  Two, of course.

Black  Two?  Why not four?

Grey  What are you talking about?

Black  The eyes of the fetus are surely part of the fetus.  But the fetus, you say, is part of mother.  So the fetus' parts are her parts, it's eyes are her eyes.

Grey  That's ridiculous!  It's crazy to say the mother has four eyes- by the same reasoning you could as well say, if the fetus is a boy, that she has a penis!

Black  Now you're catching on.

Grey.  But that's a ridiculous argument.  For heaven's sake, we don't look for amniocentesis to determine whether the mother is male or female or if the mother is brain damaged.

Black  Exactly.  But, it's not the argument that's ridiculous, it's the consequences, consequences that follow rigorously from your assumption that the fetus is a literal part of the mother.  I'm trying to help you see what you should be able to see without help.  Nobody really believes that the fetus' parts are the mother's parts- she cannot move its thumb into its mouth, or get a sensation from the sucking.  If the parts of the fetus are not parts of the mother, then neither is the fetus a literal part of mother.

Grey  It's ridiculous-a mother doesn't have four eyes.
