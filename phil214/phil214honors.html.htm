Overview

In this course we will grapple with some of the fundamental questions related to human conduct.    How we should conduct ourselves?  There are many different ways that we could respond to this question-- we will look at some of the most important approaches.  We will see that some of them are better than others. 

One of the aims of this course is to consider important aspects of some different moral theories to put us in a position to determine what a plausible moral theory might look like.  Another of our aims is to grapple with concrete moral questions that arise in disputes about lying, warfare, euthanasia and abortion. 

In Unit 1 we examine the foundations of ethical theory.  Is it plausible to think that there are objective truths in ethics that are binding for all human beings?  Once we have dealt with that preliminary question, we look at the theories of John Stuart Mill, Immanual Kant, and Thomas Aquinas to get some ideas about how to construct a moral theory.  We also apply some of these theories to questions about lying, stealing, killing in war, clitoridectomy, and other topics.

In Unit 2 we learn some of the most central concepts involved in Aristotle's virtue theory.  This theory is especially complex, so spelling it out requires more time and attention.  We will devote one unit of the course to that.

In Unit 3 we focus directly on issues such as social responsibility, just war, euthanasia, and abortion.

  • You will asked to write 2 short analytic papers on topics I will assign.  Each paper is to be written according to this format.  Each short paper is worth 10% of your final grade
  • Regular participation is critical component of this course.  Participation constitutes 10% of your final grade.
  • Each student will be responsible for one in class presentation.  More about this will be explained in class.  Presentations are worth 10% of the final grade. Grading criteria for presentations.
