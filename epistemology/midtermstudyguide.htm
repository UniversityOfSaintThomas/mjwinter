Epistemology Study Guide/Exam

 

The following is an attempt to create a guide for your studies.  Knowing the answers to the following questions is necessary, but may not be sufficient, for success on the exam.  Please come to the review session with questions, but please do not ask “Will you just go through everything?”

 

How does Socrates understand the doctrine that knowledge is perception, and why does he reject the doctrine?  How are the views of Protagoras and Heraclitus incorporated?  What are the problems with those views?

 

What is the simile of the aviary and why does it fail?  What is the simile of the wax block, and why does it fail?

 

Why does Socrates reject the idea that knowledge is true judgment?  Why does Socrates reject the idea that knowledge is true judgment with an account?  Is his argument against the latter successful?

 

What is Ayer’s definition of knowledge?  How does it differ from the classical account?  In what way does it present an externalist picture of justification?

 

Explain in detail how Gettier argues against the classical account of knowledge.  How does Gettier present his case?  What does the case presuppose?

 

How does Feldman defend Gettier against a likely challenge?  What is the challenge?  How does Feldman respond?

 

How does Goldman respond to the Gettier challenge?  What is his account of knowledge?  With what kind of knowledge does it deal?  Why?  What are some shortcomings of Goldman’s analysis?

 

What are the types of knowledge Descartes considers?  What are the doubts about each level, and how does he come to terms with these doubts?  What is the significance of the wax example?  What is the “causal principle,” how does Descartes make use of it, and what is Bonjour’s problem with it?  What is the Cartesian circle? 

 

Explain the difference between direct and representative realism about perception.  What are the strengths and shortcomings of each position?  How does Bonjour make a case for representative realism? What is Aristotle’s account of perception?  In what way might it address difficulties presented for Locke and Reid?

 

What is phenomenalism about perception and why might someone believe it?  How do Hume’s concerns about causality factor in?  How does Bonjour respond to Hume?  Why does Bonjour think that representative realism provides a better explanation of perception than phenomenalism?

 

How does Kant distinguish between analytic/synthetic judgments and between a priori/a posteriori knowledge?  How is a priori knowledge possible and what is its focus?

 

Kripke: How does Saul Kripke argue that necessary a posteriori knowledge is possible?  What about contingent a priori knowledge?  How does Saul Kripke use identity statements between proper names to argue that necessary a posteriori knowledge is possible?  How does the meter stick example suggest there is contingent a priori knowledge?  What is a serious problem with this proposal?
