Course description: Epistemology is usually understood to be the study of knowledge and justified belief. As the study of knowledge, epistemology is concerned with the following questions: What are the necessary and sufficient conditions of knowledge? What are its sources? What is its structure, and what are its limits? As the study of justified belief, epistemology aims to answer questions such as: How we are to understand the concept of justification? What makes justified beliefs justified? Is justification internal or external to one's own mind? This course will provide a systematic overview of the problems that the questions above raise and focus in some depth on issues relating to the structure and the limits of knowledge and justification.

 

Course method:

Our class discussions will be focused on presenting and evaluating arguments from assigned readings.  To make sure that students are keeping up with and making some effort to understand main points from the readings, short quizzes will be given periodically throughout the semester at the beginning of class.  If missed, these quizzes can be made up only in case of emergency or illness.

 

The midterm and final exams each cover one half of the course—the final is not cumulative.  These exams consist of true/false, multiple choice, short answer, and fill in the blank questions. 

 

A 5-page paper due near the end of the term is designed to provide an opportunity for each student to present and evaluate an argument.  More information about the paper will be provided later.
