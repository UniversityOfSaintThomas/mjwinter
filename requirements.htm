Philosophy of the Human Person

Course Description

Philosophy 115 is designed to help students acquire the skill needed to examine and evaluate arguments and texts concerned with different aspects of human nature. This course will serve its purpose if it fosters an appreciation for philosophical thought, respect for the thinkers we will study, and the desire to apply the skills and ideas learned to the area of ethics. 

The questions we will address include: Is there such a thing as truth? Do human beings have a specific nature? Are human beings free? Do human beings have a soul?  If so, could the human soul be immortal? Are human beings merely a sum of physical parts and processes? Every member of this class should leave this semester 
with the wherewithal to provide intelligent responses to these important philosophical questions. 

Our readings will be drawn from the works of some great figures in the history of thought. Most of the readings are not lengthy, but they are challenging. The positions of the philosophers we will read deserve serious thought and reflection. It is expected that every student will take the time to read and think about every assigned reading prior to each meeting. It is also expected that every student read each piece of material at least twice. 
 
 

Course Requirements
 

(1) Exams: The mid-term and final will be comprised of essay and objective questions.  You will be asked to choose from two essay questions.  You will also be given several true/false, short answer, and multiple choice questions. The first exam is worth 15% of your final grade; the second is worth 15%. 

(2) Papers: There are two short papers required for this course. Papers should be about 3 (double spaced) pages in length. Written assignments are intended to provide an opportunity for you to present and assess an argument.  Organization will be weighed heavily, and grammar will count. Each paper will be worth 15% of 
your final grade. 

(3) Class participation: Students are generally  unable to perform well in this course unless they engage themselves in the readings and discussions.  This course will be primarily based upon class discussion and Internet participation. In addition, there will be ten 5-question quizzes pertaining to logic and the 
reading assignments. Participation is worth 20% of your grade (10% in class-- regular contributions; 10% electronic-- 2 postings per week);  quizzes are worth 10%.  Assignments are worth 10% of your final grade.
