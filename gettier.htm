Analysis of Edmund Gettier’s “Is justified true belief knowledge?”

Michael Winter

 

Presuppositions:

a)      One could be justified in believing a false proposition.

b)      Justified belief is closed under logical implication

 

Gettier’s Argument:

1) If (a) and (b), then there are cases in which one could have justified true belief without knowledge.

2) If there are cases in which one could have justified true belief without knowledge, then justified true belief is not sufficient for knowledge.

3) (a) and (b)

4) Therefore, justified true belief is not sufficient for knowledge.

 

Explanation of the premises:

Premise (1) is a conditional whose truth rests on the two cases that Gettier presents.  In each of the two cases, Smith begins with a belief he is justified in holding and deduces another proposition from the initial justified belief.  Due to unusual circumstances in both cases, each of the initial beliefs turns out to be false, but the entailed beliefs are true for reasons Smith does not know.  If (a) and (b) hold, Smith has justified true belief of a different proposition in each case, but he does not know either proposition.

 

The truth of premise (2) depends on what it means to be a sufficient condition.  The three criteria from the classical account of knowledge from Plato—truth, justification, and belief—have been assumed by many to define knowledge.  If this is so, then these three criteria when taken together are sufficient for knowledge.  But if there are situations in which one could have a justified true belief without knowledge, then these conditions are not sufficient for knowledge.

 

Premise three involves two principles that function as presuppositions in the argument. (a) seems to be justified easily.  I can believe with justification that I will be in this room in one minute, and that could turn out to be false.  Countless other examples support this principle.  (b) has a lot of intuitive support.  If I am justified in believing that my name is “Winter”, and I see that this implies that I have I name, then I am justified in believing that I have a name.

 

Critique:

To reject Gettier’s argument, which seems fishy in some way, one much either identify a false premise or show that the conclusion does not follow from the premises.  The conclusion does indeed follow.  Which premise is false?  It is hard to say, but I think (b) in premise (3) is most vulnerable.  Consider this example.  I am justified in believing that UST is a university and this implies any and every necessary truth.  Take some complicated mathematical truth, t.  Even though I can barely understand t, my belief about UST entails it, and therefore by (b) I am justified in believing t.  This might be problematic.
