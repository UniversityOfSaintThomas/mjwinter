This course focuses on some of the great ideas about the nature of moral thought.  We consider three normative ethical theories that have been at the forefront of the discussion of ethics in the west: utilitarianism, deontology, and virtue ethics.  In each case, we begin by reading selections from classical authors before reading contributions from 20th century authors.  This procedure will give us a good sense for how each of these approaches has developed.  The course will also deal with meta-ethical themes such as the is/ought problem, moral realism and non-cognitivism.

Grading

Since this a 400 level seminar, a good portion of the course is based on in-class discussion.  Regular attendance is expected.  There will be no exams, and students in the course work toward a final presentation at the end of the semester that will be attended by some philosophy faculty.  Outside philosophy faculty members evaluate presentations only for the purposes of departmental assessment, which has no bearing on your grade.  I will grade the presentation as part of the course though. 

In addition to the final presentation, each student is responsible for one other in class presentation.  More about the guidelines for presentations will be said as the course develops. 

There will be one paper of approximately 10 pages in length due at the end of the semester.   I will say more about the guidelines for papers as the course develops. 

 

Here is the breakdown of the grade for the course:

Class participation:        25%

Final paper:                  50%

2 presentations:             50%
